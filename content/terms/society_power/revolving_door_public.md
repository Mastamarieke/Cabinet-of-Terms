# Revolving Door

The regulator becomes the lobbyist. The general becomes the board member. The minister becomes the vice president.

**Literal meaning**
*Revolving Door* — a door that turns continuously, allowing movement in both directions. Describes the movement of individuals between government positions and the private sector they were responsible for regulating.

**Origin**
The term has been used in political science since the 1970s. In the tech sector, it is documented in movement between Silicon Valley and government positions in the FTC, FCC, DoD, and intelligence agencies.

> The Revolving Door is the structural movement between regulatory government positions and the industries being regulated — creating conflicts of interest that systematically weaken oversight.

**The Appeal**
Industry expertise is valuable in regulatory positions. Attracting capable people to government service requires competitive compensation — which the revolving door provides.

**The Friction**
The conflict of interest is structural: regulators who expect industry employment after government service have incentives to regulate lightly. The [[terms/society_power/military_industrial_complex_public|Military-Industrial Complex]] depends on the revolving door as its personnel mechanism.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see the Revolving Door as a structural feature of regulatory capture, you see that the question is not whether regulators can be trusted — it is what structures create the conditions for trustworthy oversight.
