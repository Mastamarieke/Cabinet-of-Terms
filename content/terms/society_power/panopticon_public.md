# Panopticon

You do not know if you are being watched right now. That is the point.

**Literal meaning**
*Pan* — all. *Opticon* — see. Jeremy Bentham's 1791 prison design: a circular building with a central observation tower where all cells are visible — but prisoners cannot see whether the guard is present. Effect: prisoners regulate themselves.

**Origin**
Jeremy Bentham designed the Panopticon in 1791. Michel Foucault analysed it in *Discipline and Punish* (1975) as a diagram of modern power: the internalisation of surveillance that produces self-regulation without requiring constant watching.

> The Panopticon is Foucault's model of modern power — surveillance that produces self-regulation by making the possibility of observation constant and the certainty of it unknowable.

**The Appeal**
Foucault's analysis is foundational for understanding how power operates in digital environments. The Panopticon is a precise model for social media self-presentation: you perform for an audience you cannot fully see.

**The Friction**
[[public/society_power/surveillance_capitalism_public|Surveillance Capitalism]] is the Panopticon extended to commercial contexts. [[public/behaviour_relationships/cognitive_surrender_public|Cognitive Surrender]] is the contemporary form of panoptic self-regulation: internalising the judgments of algorithms whose criteria are unknown.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see the Panopticon as a working model of digital social life, you see that self-censorship and self-presentation are not personal anxieties — they are designed effects.
