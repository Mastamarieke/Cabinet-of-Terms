# Nuclear Renaissance

Tech companies are reopening nuclear power plants. Not because governments asked them to — because AI needs the electricity.

**Literal meaning:** The nuclear renaissance refers to a renewed interest in nuclear energy as a large-scale, low-carbon power source — after decades of decline following Chernobyl (1986) and Fukushima (2011). In the AI context, the term specifically describes the tech industry's role as the primary financial driver of this revival, through direct investment in new reactors and long-term power purchase agreements with existing plants.

**Origin:** The term "nuclear renaissance" was first used in the early 2000s in energy policy discussions, before stalling after Fukushima. Its reappearance from 2023 onward is structurally different: it is being led not by governments but by technology companies — Microsoft, Google, Amazon — facing electricity demands that renewable sources alone cannot currently meet at the required scale and reliability.

> The tech industry's financially driven revival of nuclear energy, motivated primarily by the electricity demands of AI infrastructure rather than by public energy policy.

**The Appeal:** Nuclear energy produces large amounts of low-carbon electricity reliably, without the intermittency of wind and solar. For companies with [[Greenwashing]]-sensitive sustainability pledges and rapidly growing energy needs, nuclear is a genuinely attractive solution. For climate advocates who support nuclear as a necessary part of decarbonisation, tech investment provides welcome momentum.

**The Friction:** The nuclear renaissance reframes a public infrastructure question as a corporate procurement decision. Nuclear power plants involve long time horizons, significant waste management challenges, and risks that are ultimately borne by the communities and states where they are built — not by the companies signing the power agreements. [[Sacrifice Zones]] — areas bearing disproportionate environmental burdens of digital infrastructure — may expand to include the regions hosting reactors financed by AI demand. The energy transition is real; who controls it, and for whose purposes, is a different question.

**Why This Matters:** The nuclear renaissance makes visible who is setting the terms of the energy transition. When a technology company's infrastructure demand becomes large enough to reshape national energy policy, the line between private interest and public infrastructure disappears. That disappearance deserves a name.
