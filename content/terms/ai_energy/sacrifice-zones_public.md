# Sacrifice Zones

Somewhere, a community is living with the environmental cost of infrastructure they do not use and did not choose.

**Literal meaning:** Sacrifice zones are geographic areas — typically low-income, politically marginalised, or sparsely regulated — that bear concentrated and disproportionate environmental burdens from industrial or infrastructural activity. In the AI & Energy context, the term describes communities near data centres, power plants, or resource extraction sites whose local environment is degraded to sustain digital services used elsewhere.

**Origin:** The term originates in environmental justice scholarship and activism, associated with researchers such as Robert Bullard and Steve Lerner, who documented patterns in which environmental harm from industrial facilities was systematically concentrated in communities of colour and working-class communities in the United States. From the 2010s onward, it has been extended to digital infrastructure — data centres, semiconductor factories, rare earth mining — by scholars working at the intersection of environmental justice and platform studies.

> Geographic areas that bear concentrated environmental costs — pollution, water depletion, land use — from AI infrastructure serving populations elsewhere, with limited political capacity to resist or redirect that burden.

**The Appeal:** The term is not primarily descriptive of a corporate choice — it names a structural outcome that emerges from market forces, regulatory gaps, and political asymmetries that no single actor fully controls. Acknowledging sacrifice zones does not require attributing conscious malice. It describes a pattern.

**The Friction:** Patterns are reproduced by choices. [[Externalized Costs]] — environmental burdens absorbed by communities rather than priced into AI services — make it rational for companies to locate infrastructure where resistance is lowest and land is cheapest. [[Digital Colonialism]] describes the international dimension of the same logic. What is called economic development from one angle is sacrifice from another — and the people doing the sacrificing rarely get to choose the name. [[Greenwashing]] allows the companies benefiting from sacrifice zones to maintain a sustainability reputation that does not extend to the communities around their infrastructure.

**Why This Matters:** Sacrifice zone is a term that refuses geographical abstraction. It insists that digital infrastructure has a physical address, and that address is in someone's neighbourhood. Once you know the term, "the cloud" becomes a location — not a metaphor.
