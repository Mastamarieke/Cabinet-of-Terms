# Carbon Footprint of AI

Before you read this sentence, a server somewhere consumed electricity to generate it.

**Literal meaning:** The total CO₂ emissions attributable to an AI system — calculated per query, per training run, or per model — representing AI's measurable contribution to climate change.

**Origin:** The concept of a carbon footprint was popularised in the early 2000s, partly by a BP marketing campaign that shifted responsibility for emissions from corporations to individuals. Applied to AI, it was sharpened by Strubell et al.'s 2019 measurements and extended by researchers at Google and elsewhere who began publishing emissions figures for specific models — figures that vary enormously depending on the energy source used by the data centre.

> A measure of the greenhouse gas emissions produced at each stage of an AI system's life — from training to deployment to every individual use.

**The Appeal:** The term gives something previously invisible a number. Numbers enable comparison, accountability, and pressure. Researchers, journalists, and regulators have used carbon footprint estimates to demand transparency from AI companies — and some companies have responded by publishing emissions data. The frame works.

**The Friction:** The frame also has a design flaw. [[Greenwashing]] — sustainability rhetoric deployed as marketing without changing underlying behaviour — exploits the same vocabulary. A company can publish a carbon footprint figure while simultaneously tripling its energy consumption. The metric becomes a communication tool. More structurally: carbon footprint calculations depend entirely on what you count and where you draw the boundary. Training emissions are often disclosed; [[Inference Cost]] — the energy consumed per individual query — rarely is. The iceberg stays submerged.

**Why This Matters:** The term teaches a specific reading practice: ask not just *what is the number* but *what does the number include*. A disclosed footprint is not the same as a small one. Accounting choices are political choices. Once you see the boundary, you can ask who drew it.
