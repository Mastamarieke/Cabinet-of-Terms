# Externalized Costs

When a company does not pay for the damage it causes, someone else does. That someone is usually a community near infrastructure, a future generation, or both.

**Literal meaning:** In economics, an externality is a cost (or benefit) that is not reflected in the price of a transaction but is borne by a third party. Externalized costs are costs that companies transfer — deliberately or structurally — onto the broader environment, society, or specific communities, without compensation. In the AI & Energy context, the term describes the gap between what AI services cost to provide (at the company level) and what they cost to exist (at the societal level).

**Origin:** The concept of externalities was formalised by economist Arthur Pigou in the 1920s, and "negative externalities" became central to environmental economics from the 1960s onward. Applied to digital infrastructure, the term was brought into AI critique primarily through the work of scholars tracking the material conditions of the tech industry — including Kate Crawford's *Atlas of AI* (2021), which systematically documented the environmental, labour, and social costs absent from AI's price tags.

> The gap between what AI companies pay for their infrastructure and what AI infrastructure actually costs — a gap filled by communities, ecosystems, and future populations who bear environmental harm without compensation.

**The Appeal:** Externalisation is not an aberration — it is how markets function when costs are not regulated. Companies that internalise all their environmental costs price themselves out of competition. The economic logic is coherent even when the outcome is damaging. This is why the concept requires regulatory response rather than only corporate responsibility.

**The Friction:** AI companies operate at a scale where externalized costs are enormous in absolute terms, even when small per transaction. [[Sacrifice Zones]] exist because it is cheaper — in regulatory and financial terms — to locate infrastructure near communities with limited political capacity to resist. [[Greenwashing]] is partly a response to the reputational risk of visible externalisation: the sustainability pledge manages the optics of costs that have not actually been absorbed. [[Digital Colonialism]] describes the international dimension: costs travel to where resistance is lowest, benefit flows to where purchasing power is highest.

**Why This Matters:** Externalized costs make visible the structural relationship between price and harm. The AI service that appears free or cheap is internalising only a fraction of its actual cost. Once you know to ask *who is paying the rest*, the market price of a technology becomes one figure among several — and not the most important one.
