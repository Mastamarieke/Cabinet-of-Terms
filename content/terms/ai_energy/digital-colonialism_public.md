# Digital Colonialism

A data centre is built in a country with cheap land, cheap labour, and weak environmental regulation. The electricity it uses does not reach local households. The AI it powers serves users elsewhere.

**Literal meaning:** Digital colonialism describes a structural relationship in which digital infrastructure — data centres, undersea cables, platform economies — extracts resources, labour, and data from lower-income countries or regions while the economic and technological benefits flow primarily to wealthier ones. In the AI & Energy context, the term focuses specifically on the energy and environmental dimension of that extraction.

**Origin:** The term draws on postcolonial theory and dependency economics, applied to digital systems. Scholars including Payal Arora, Nick Couldry, and Ulises Mejias have developed the concept since the mid-2010s. In the AI energy debate, it gained sharper specificity as researchers documented the geographic concentration of data centre construction in regions with regulatory gaps and resource abundance — while AI services remained geographically remote from those regions.

> A pattern in which AI infrastructure consumes local resources — energy, water, land — in lower-income regions, while the services and profits generated by that infrastructure benefit populations elsewhere.

**The Appeal:** Data centres bring jobs, investment, and infrastructure to regions that may lack them. Host governments actively court these investments. The framing of mutual benefit is not always empty — employment, grid upgrades, and local supplier chains are real.

**The Friction:** The structural asymmetry persists beneath the mutual-benefit rhetoric. [[Externalized Costs]] — environmental burdens placed on communities rather than absorbed by companies — concentrate in the host region. [[Sacrifice Zones]] — areas bearing disproportionate environmental burdens of digital infrastructure — are selected in part because political capacity to resist is lower. Data about local populations is often part of the extraction: training datasets draw from global commons while serving predominantly wealthy users. The colonial analogy is not decorative — it names a specific pattern of resource flow.

**Why This Matters:** Digital colonialism asks where the infrastructure lives and who benefits. Once you know the term, "cloud" stops being a neutral metaphor. Clouds are in specific places, drawing on specific resources, operating under specific regulatory conditions — chosen for reasons that have everything to do with power.
