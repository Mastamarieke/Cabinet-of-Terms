# Content Moderator

Every piece of content you did not see today was reviewed by someone. That someone is not doing well.

**Literal meaning:** A content moderator is a person employed — directly or through subcontractors — to review user-generated content on digital platforms and remove material that violates platform policies: graphic violence, child abuse, hate speech, self-harm, and more. Moderation happens at enormous scale: billions of pieces of content are posted daily, and the fraction requiring human review still numbers in the millions.

**Origin:** Content moderation as a formal profession emerged with the scaling of social media platforms in the 2000s and 2010s. The conditions of the work were largely invisible until investigative reporting — most notably by journalists Casey Newton and Muyi Xiao — documented the psychological damage sustained by moderators at companies including Facebook, YouTube, and their subcontractors. A 2020 lawsuit by former Facebook moderators resulted in a $52 million settlement for trauma-related damages.

> A person employed to review and remove harmful content from digital platforms — performing invisible, psychologically damaging labour that makes platforms habitable for others.

**The Appeal:** Content moderation is genuinely necessary. Without it, platforms would be overwhelmed by the worst of human behaviour, and the communities that use them — including vulnerable people — would be exposed to serious harm. The profession has real expertise: skilled moderators develop knowledge of extremist networks, abuse patterns, and manipulation tactics that informs platform policy and law enforcement.

**The Friction:** The conditions under which the work is performed contradict the safety it is meant to provide. [[Ghost Worker]] — Gray and Suri's term for the invisible human labour behind AI and platform systems — describes the structural invisibility: content moderation is hidden behind contractor layers, NDAs, and the platform's preference for the appearance of automated safety. Moderators are paid minimum wage or close to it, given inadequate psychological support, and exposed to content that causes documented PTSD and secondary trauma. [[Attention Harvesting]] — the systematic extraction of human attention for commercial purposes — is the economic model the moderator makes sustainable, while bearing its costs. The platform profits; the moderator processes what the platform's design attracts.

**Why This Matters:** Content moderation makes visible the human body at the bottom of the automated safety stack. The frictionless experience of a platform is maintained by people in difficult conditions. Once you know the profession exists, the platform's claims about AI-powered safety become a sentence with a human cost attached.
