# Predatory Design

The game is free. The coin pack is £4.99. You have already spent £80 this month.

**Literal meaning**
*Predatory* — exploiting vulnerability for gain. *Design* — the intentional structuring of an experience. Predatory Design describes deliberate design choices that exploit psychological vulnerabilities to maximise spending or time.

**Origin**
The term is most associated with gaming — free-to-play mobile games that use loot boxes, artificial scarcity, and social pressure to drive in-app purchases. It has expanded to describe design practices across platforms, apps, and subscription services.

> Predatory Design is the deliberate use of psychological vulnerabilities — loss aversion, social pressure, sunk cost — to maximise user spending or engagement beyond their intended limits.

**The Appeal**
Free-to-play games are accessible to everyone. The base experience is often genuinely enjoyable.

**The Friction**
[[terms/platform_mechanisms/dark_patterns_public|Dark Patterns]] — interface design that misleads users — and [[terms/platform_mechanisms/gamification_creep_public|Gamification Creep]] — game logic entering non-game contexts — combine in predatory design. Loot boxes apply gambling mechanics to children. Artificial scarcity creates urgency. Social features create obligation. The design targets the psychological architecture of its most vulnerable users.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see predatory design as a deliberate strategy rather than a series of aggressive business decisions, you see that the vulnerability being exploited was mapped, measured, and monetised.
