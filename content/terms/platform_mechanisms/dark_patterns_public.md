# Dark Patterns

You meant to cancel the subscription. The button was grey and very small. The confirmation took four more steps.

**Literal meaning**
*Dark* — obscured, hidden, working against the user. *Patterns* — recurring design solutions. Dark Patterns are interface design choices that systematically work against user interests.

**Origin**
UX researcher Harry Brignull coined the term in 2010 and catalogued common examples: roach motels, trick questions, hidden costs, confirm-shaming. The EU's Digital Services Act (2022) later incorporated "deceptive design" — the regulatory preferred term — as a prohibited practice.

> Dark Patterns are deliberate interface design choices that mislead, manipulate, or coerce users into actions that serve platform interests over their own.

**The Appeal**
There is no appeal from the user perspective — dark patterns are designed to be invisible until they have already worked.

**The Friction**
[[public/platform_mechanisms/nudging_public|Nudging]] — steering behaviour through environmental design — becomes a dark pattern when the nudge is deceptive rather than transparent. The line between persuasion and manipulation is structural: does the design serve the user's stated goal or circumvent it?

**Why This Matters**
Once you see dark patterns as deliberate design rather than bad UX, you stop blaming yourself for clicking the wrong thing and start asking who designed the interface and what they wanted you to do.
