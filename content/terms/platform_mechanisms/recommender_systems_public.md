# Recommender Systems

You did not choose the next video. The next video chose you.

**Literal meaning**
*Recommender* — a system that suggests. *Systems* — infrastructure. Recommender systems are algorithms that determine what content appears in your feed, your search results, your homepage.

**Origin**
Recommender systems emerged from collaborative filtering research in the 1990s. Amazon deployed early versions for product recommendations. Netflix, YouTube, and Spotify built large-scale systems in the 2000s. By 2018, YouTube publicly acknowledged that its recommender system drove approximately 70% of all viewing time.

> Recommender systems are algorithms that select and sequence content based on engagement signals — optimised not for what you want but for what keeps you on the platform.

**The Appeal**
Discovery. The algorithm surfaces content you would not have found yourself — music, films, ideas, communities. That is real and often valuable.

**The Friction**
Optimising for engagement does not optimise for satisfaction, accuracy, or wellbeing. Content that provokes strong emotions — anger, fear, outrage — performs better than content that informs calmly. [[terms/platform_mechanisms/attention_harvesting_public|Attention Harvesting]] — the systematic extraction of attention for commercial purposes — depends on recommender systems as its primary instrument.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see recommender systems as the infrastructure of attention harvesting, you see that the question "what should I watch next?" has already been answered — by a system with different interests than yours.
