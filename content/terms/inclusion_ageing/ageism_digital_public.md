# Ageism (Digital)

The interface was not designed to exclude older users. It was designed without them. The effect is the same.

**Literal meaning**
*Ageism* — prejudice or discrimination against people on the grounds of age. *Digital* — applied to digital systems, interfaces, and policies. Digital Ageism is age discrimination embedded in technology design, digital policy, and the language used to describe older technology users.

**Origin**
Robert Butler coined "ageism" in 1969 by analogy with racism and sexism. Applied to digital contexts, it describes the systematic design of technology for younger users, the assumption that older adults are inherently less capable of technology use, and the structural exclusion produced by these assumptions.

> Digital Ageism is age discrimination embedded in technology design and policy — producing exclusion through assumptions about who technology is for.

**The Appeal**
Identifying Digital Ageism as a specific form of discrimination enables targeted interventions and legal challenges.

**The Friction**
Digital Ageism is often unintentional: design teams skew young, user testing pools skew young, and the result is interfaces optimised for younger users without deliberate exclusion of older ones. The effect is structural exclusion without discriminatory intent. [[terms/inclusion_ageing/tech_abandonment_public|Tech Abandonment]] is the documented consequence: older users giving up on technology that was not designed for them.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see Digital Ageism as a structural feature of technology development rather than individual prejudice, you see that the solution is not sensitivity training — it is changing who is in the room when technology is designed and tested.
