# Predatory Design

The game knows the child is seven. It also knows which notifications bring them back, which rewards make them spend, and how long they will play before a parent intervenes. This knowledge was designed in.

**Literal meaning:** Predatory design describes interface and product design choices that deliberately exploit users' psychological vulnerabilities to maximise engagement, spending, or data extraction — with particular severity when those users are children, whose developmental stage makes them more susceptible to specific manipulation techniques. The term is the child-specific intensification of the broader concept of dark patterns.

**Origin:** The term developed in parallel with broader dark patterns discourse in the 2010s, gaining specific child-focused application as regulatory bodies — the UK's Information Commissioner's Office, the US Federal Trade Commission, and the EU — began investigating design practices in children's apps, games, and platforms. The Age Appropriate Design Code (UK, 2021) was the first regulatory framework to specifically address predatory design targeting children, requiring platforms to configure their highest privacy settings as defaults for child users.

> Design that deliberately exploits children's developmental vulnerabilities — their impulsivity, their susceptibility to social pressure, their incomplete understanding of money and time — to extract engagement, spending, or data.

**The Appeal:** Platform and game designers do not typically describe their work as predatory. The design choices that maximise engagement — variable rewards, social features, notifications, progress systems — are the same choices that make products compelling and enjoyable. The line between engaging design and exploitative design is genuinely contested, and many designers believe their products offer value to children.

**The Friction:** The line is less contested when children are the target population. Children's cognitive development — specifically their executive function, impulse control, and understanding of financial value — differs from adults' in documented ways. [[Dopamine Feedback Loops]] — variable reward systems — exploit impulsivity that is developmentally normal in children and that adults have more capacity to regulate. [[Loot Boxes]] target the same vulnerability. [[Roblox]] and similar platforms use social pressure — avatar appearance, in-game status, peer visibility — that is particularly effective in adolescents for whom social belonging is a primary developmental concern. [[Dark Patterns]] in the broader sense are the design vocabulary; predatory design names their application to a population that cannot fully evaluate what is being done to them. [[Screen Time]] — the dominant parental response — addresses the symptom without addressing the cause: the design itself.

**Why This Matters:** Predatory design shifts responsibility. When the problem is framed as screen time, the parent manages it. When the problem is framed as predatory design, the designer answers for it. The framing determines who is accountable.
