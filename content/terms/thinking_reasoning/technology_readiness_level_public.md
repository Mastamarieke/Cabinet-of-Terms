# Technology Readiness Level

The technology is described as ready. The question is: ready by whose scale, and for what?

**Literal meaning**
*Technology* — applied technical knowledge. *Readiness* — degree of preparation for deployment. *Level* — a point on a defined scale. TRL is a nine-point scale developed by NASA to assess how mature a technology is for deployment.

**Origin**
NASA developed the Technology Readiness Level scale in the 1970s to standardise assessments of spacecraft technology maturity. The EU adopted it for research funding frameworks (Horizon programmes). It has since been applied in defence, industry, and increasingly in AI and digital technology policy.

> Technology Readiness Level is a standardised scale for assessing technological maturity — from basic research (TRL 1) to proven deployment (TRL 9).

**The Appeal**
TRL provides a shared vocabulary for assessing whether a technology is ready for investment, regulation, or deployment. It reduces ambiguity in funding and procurement decisions.

**The Friction**
TRL was designed for hardware systems with clear performance criteria. Applied to AI and social technologies — where "readiness" includes social, ethical, and political dimensions — the scale does not capture what matters most. A system can be TRL 9 technically while being deeply unready socially. The scale's authority can suppress legitimate concerns by framing them as non-technical.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see TRL as a framing instrument rather than a neutral assessment, you see that declaring a technology "ready" is not just a technical judgement — it is a political one that determines who bears the consequences of deployment.
