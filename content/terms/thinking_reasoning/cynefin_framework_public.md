# Cynefin Framework

Not all problems work the same way. Some have right answers. Some have best practices. Some have no answers — only emergent responses. Cynefin helps you tell them apart.

**Literal meaning**
*Cynefin* — Welsh word for habitat or place; the context that shapes who we are. The Cynefin Framework is a decision-making model that distinguishes between different types of problems and the appropriate responses to each.

**Origin**
Dave Snowden developed Cynefin at IBM in 1999, drawing on complexity science, anthropology, and cognitive science. The framework distinguishes five domains: Clear (best practices apply), Complicated (expert analysis), Complex (emergent practice), Chaotic (act first), and Confused (unknown domain). It has been applied in management, policy, and education.

> The Cynefin Framework is a sense-making model that helps distinguish between types of problems — enabling appropriate rather than habitual responses.

**The Appeal**
Cynefin names a common frustration: applying simple solutions to complex problems, or over-complicating clear ones. The framework gives vocabulary to a pattern most practitioners recognise.

**The Friction**
The framework is a diagnostic tool, not a solution generator. Misapplication is common — particularly the tendency to classify everything as complex to avoid accountability for clear decisions. In media and platform analysis, most phenomena occupy the Complex domain: causes and effects are only visible in retrospect.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see Cynefin as a thinking instrument rather than a management framework, you see that the question "what kind of problem is this?" is prior to any attempt to solve it — and that digital media phenomena almost always resist simple causal explanations.
