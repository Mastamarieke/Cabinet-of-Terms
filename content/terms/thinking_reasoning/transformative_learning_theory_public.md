# Transformative Learning Theory

You did not just learn a new fact. Something shifted in how you see. That is a different kind of learning.

**Literal meaning**
*Transformative* — producing fundamental change. *Learning* — acquiring knowledge or skills. *Theory* — a systematic framework for understanding. Transformative Learning Theory describes learning that changes the frame through which experience is interpreted.

**Origin**
Jack Mezirow developed Transformative Learning Theory from his 1970s research on women returning to higher education after long absences. He identified that the most significant learning was not the acquisition of new information but the transformation of the meaning perspectives through which information is interpreted. The theory has become foundational in adult education.

> Transformative Learning Theory is Mezirow's account of learning as perspective transformation — the process by which critical reflection challenges and changes the assumptions that shape how we see the world.

**The Appeal**
The theory names something that effective educators recognise: the most durable learning is not additive but reconstructive. New information that fits existing frames is easier to absorb — but information that challenges the frame produces deeper change.

**The Friction**
Perspective transformation is uncomfortable and often resisted. The disorienting dilemma — Mezirow's term for the experience that triggers transformative learning — is not a pleasant starting point. Educational contexts that prioritise comfort over challenge systematically avoid the conditions that produce transformation.

**Why This Matters**
Transformative Learning Theory is the theoretical foundation of Project Digitale Alertheid. The vault is designed not to add information about digital media but to shift the frame through which that media is seen. Every term analysis is, in Mezirow's terms, an invitation to a disorienting dilemma.
