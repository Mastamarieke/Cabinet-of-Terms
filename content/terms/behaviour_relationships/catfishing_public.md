# Catfishing

The profile was attractive, articulate, interested in everything you were interested in. It took three months to discover it was a fabrication.

**Literal meaning**
Origin disputed — popularised by the 2010 documentary *Catfish*. A catfish is someone who creates a false online identity to deceive others, typically for emotional manipulation or financial gain.

**Origin**
The term entered mainstream use after Nev Schulman's 2010 documentary documented his own experience of being deceived by a fabricated online identity. The MTV series *Catfish* (2012–) systematised the format. The practice predates the term, but digital platforms reduced the effort required to maintain false identities.

> Catfishing is the construction and maintenance of a false online identity to deceive others — enabled by platform architectures that do not require identity verification.

**The Appeal**
There is no legitimate appeal from the victim's perspective, but catfishing often exploits real needs: loneliness, desire for connection, the wish to be seen.

**The Friction**
Platforms that do not verify identity make catfishing structurally easy. [[terms/platform_mechanisms/microtargeting_public|Microtargeting]] logic — understanding what specific individuals respond to — can be applied maliciously: a skilled catfish learns what the target wants and provides it. The harm is not just deception but the exploitation of genuine vulnerability.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see catfishing as a structural consequence of unverified identity platforms rather than individual pathology, you see that the question is not just about bad actors — it is about what architectures enable.
