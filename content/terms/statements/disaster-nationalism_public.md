# Disaster Nationalism

The crisis is real. The solution offered is: close the borders, expel the outsiders, return to a purer order. The disaster is not a problem to be solved — it is a resource to be used.

**Literal meaning:** Disaster nationalism describes the political strategy of using genuine crises — economic collapse, pandemic, climate disaster, mass migration — to consolidate nationalist and authoritarian power. The term builds on Naomi Klein's concept of disaster capitalism (the *Shock Doctrine*, 2007), extended to the domain of political identity: crisis as the moment when extraordinary measures become possible and populations become receptive to radical simplifications of complex problems.

**Origin:** The concept is associated with the work of Naomi Klein and, in its digital dimensions, with researchers studying how platform algorithms amplify crisis-driven political mobilisation. It gained analytical sharpness in the 2010s and 2020s as a series of genuine crises — financial crash, pandemic, climate events, migration — coincided with the rise of nationalist political movements that explicitly framed themselves as responses to those crises.

> The political strategy of using genuine crises to consolidate nationalist power — where disaster is not a problem to be managed collectively but a resource for mobilising exclusionary politics.

**The Appeal:** Disaster nationalism responds to real experiences of disruption. When institutions fail, when communities feel abandoned, when crisis is genuine and the response is inadequate — the offer of a clear enemy and a clear solution has genuine appeal. The nationalist frame provides meaning, belonging, and a sense of agency in situations where collective institutions have demonstrably failed to provide them.

**The Friction:** The mechanism is the problem. Crisis is used not to understand its causes but to redirect anger toward available targets — immigrants, minorities, political opponents, international institutions. [[Millenarianism]] — end-times thinking as political-religious structure — provides the eschatological urgency that makes the redirect feel necessary. [[Endtime Fascism]] — fascism legitimised by apocalyptic urgency — is the terminus of the logic when it passes through institutional power. [[Armageddon Complex]] — apocalyptic thinking that justifies power accumulation — names the self-serving dimension: the people who benefit from the crisis framing are rarely those bearing its costs. [[Dark MAGA]] is one contemporary instance of the aesthetic through which this mechanism circulates online.

**Why This Matters:** Disaster nationalism teaches a specific reading practice for political crises: ask who benefits from the frame, not just from the crisis. The disaster and the political use of the disaster are distinct — and the second is often designed to prevent resolution of the first.
