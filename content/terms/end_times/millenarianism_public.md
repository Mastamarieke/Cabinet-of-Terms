# Millenarianism

Every era has its version: the world as it is is corrupt and ending; a radically better world is coming; a chosen group will survive the transition and inherit what follows. The content changes. The structure does not.

**Literal meaning:** Millenarianism is the belief — found across religious traditions and political movements — that a current corrupt order will be destroyed and replaced by a radically transformed world, often following a period of catastrophe. The term derives from Christian eschatology and the concept of a thousand-year reign, but the structure appears across cultures and historical periods. In its secular form, it describes any ideology organised around the imminent collapse of the present order and the emergence of something fundamentally new.

**Origin:** The concept is central to the history of religions and was analysed sociologically by Norman Cohn in *The Pursuit of the Millennium* (1957) and anthropologically by scholars including Kenelm Burridge. Its application to contemporary tech culture has been developed by researchers who identify structural similarities between religious end-times movements and secular ideologies like [[e/acc (Effective Accelerationism)]], longtermism, and AI doomerism — all of which share the basic millenarian structure: current order failing, catastrophe approaching, a chosen community prepared to navigate the transition.

> The belief structure — religious or secular — organised around the imminent end of the current order and the arrival of a radically transformed world, with a chosen community positioned to survive and shape what comes next.

**The Appeal:** Millenarian thinking addresses a genuine psychological need: the need to make sense of disorder, to locate oneself in a meaningful narrative, and to belong to a community that understands what others do not. In periods of genuine disruption — technological, political, ecological — the millenarian frame provides coherence. It is also motivating: if the stakes are civilisational, then ordinary actions carry extraordinary significance.

**The Friction:** The structure is politically dangerous regardless of its content. Once the present order is framed as irredeemably corrupt and collapse as imminent, ordinary political accountability dissolves — the normal rules of evidence, procedure, and harm prevention no longer apply to those preparing for what comes next. [[Armageddon Complex]] — apocalyptic thinking that justifies power accumulation — is millenarianism in the hands of those with resources to act on it. [[Endtime Fascism]] — fascism legitimised by eschatological urgency — is its most extreme political expression. [[Disaster Nationalism]] is its contemporary mass-market form. [[Doomsday Prep for the Super-Rich]] is its consumer product.

**Why This Matters:** Millenarianism is the deep grammar of end-times discourse. Once you recognise the structure — corrupt present, catastrophic transition, chosen community, transformed future — it becomes visible across ideologically diverse movements that appear to have nothing in common.
