# VSD (Value Sensitive Design)

Before building the system, someone asks: whose values does this embed — and who did not get a seat at the table?

**Literal meaning**
*Value* — what matters to people and communities. *Sensitive* — responsive, attentive. *Design* — the intentional structuring of systems and artefacts. VSD is a design methodology that systematically considers human values throughout the design process.

**Origin**
Batya Friedman and Peter Kahn developed Value Sensitive Design at the University of Washington from the 1990s onward. The methodology proposes conceptual, empirical, and technical investigations to identify and respond to the values of stakeholders — including those not represented in the design process.

> Value Sensitive Design is a methodology for systematically integrating human values into technology design — treating values not as constraints but as design requirements.

**The Appeal**
VSD offers designers a concrete framework for ethical practice. It names what is often implicit — that design embeds values — and makes it visible and actionable.

**The Friction**
VSD is a methodology, not a guarantee. Identifying values does not resolve conflicts between them. Corporate adoption can reduce VSD to compliance theatre — the appearance of value consideration without structural change. [[terms/design_philosophy/fair_patterns_public|Fair Patterns]] — the structural counterpart to dark patterns — operationalises similar intentions at the interface level.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see VSD as a design practice rather than a checklist, you see that the question "whose values?" is always also the question "who has power?" — and that design without that question reproduces existing power structures.
