# Fair Patterns

The cancel button is the same size as the confirm button. The default is the option that serves you. This is a design choice.

**Literal meaning**
*Fair* — just, equitable, transparent. *Patterns* — recurring design solutions. Fair Patterns are interface design choices that serve user interests — the structural counterpart to dark patterns.

**Origin**
Fair Patterns emerged as a design concept in the mid-2010s as researchers and practitioners documented dark patterns and sought to articulate their opposite. The term gained traction through UX research, consumer protection discourse, and the work of organisations including the European Data Protection Board.

> Fair Patterns are interface design choices that serve user interests rather than circumvent them — the same structural logic as dark patterns, applied with opposite intent.

**The Appeal**
Fair Patterns demonstrate that the design mechanisms used for manipulation can be redirected. The same knowledge of cognitive architecture that enables dark patterns enables fair ones.

**The Friction**
Fair Patterns are commercially disadvantaged in the same way as [[terms/design_philosophy/calm_technology_public|Calm Technology]]: they generate less engagement, fewer accidental purchases, and lower conversion rates than their manipulative counterparts. Adoption requires regulatory pressure or genuine value alignment — and [[terms/platform_mechanisms/dark_patterns_public|Dark Patterns]] remain the market default.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see Fair Patterns as a design practice rather than an ideal, you see that ethical interface design is technically straightforward — the barrier is not knowledge but incentive.
