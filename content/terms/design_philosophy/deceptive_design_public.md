# Deceptive Design

Dark patterns is the term you know. Deceptive design is the term that holds up in court.

**Literal meaning**
*Deceptive* — intentionally misleading. *Design* — the structured creation of systems and interfaces. Deceptive Design is the regulatory and academic preferred term for interface patterns that mislead or manipulate users.

**Origin**
The EU's Digital Services Act (2022) and consumer protection frameworks adopted "deceptive design" as the regulatory term, replacing the more informal "dark patterns." The shift reflects legal precision: "deceptive" implies intent and consumer harm, making enforcement clearer than the metaphorical "dark."

> Deceptive Design is the legally and academically preferred term for interface design that misleads users — carrying enforcement weight that the informal "dark patterns" does not.

**The Appeal**
The terminological precision matters: naming something "deceptive" rather than "dark" assigns responsibility and enables legal remedy.

**The Friction**
[[terms/platform_mechanisms/dark_patterns_public|Dark Patterns]] — the informal term — remains more widely recognised in public discourse. The regulatory term has not yet shifted popular vocabulary. [[terms/design_philosophy/fair_patterns_public|Fair Patterns]] — the direct structural counterpart — operationalises the alternative.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see Deceptive Design as a legal category rather than a design criticism, you see that the naming of practices as deceptive is not academic — it is the precondition for enforcement.
