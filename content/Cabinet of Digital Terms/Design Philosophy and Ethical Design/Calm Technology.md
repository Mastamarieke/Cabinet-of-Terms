# Calm Technology


***The technology is there when you need it. When you do not, you do not notice it.***

**Literal meaning:** **Calm technology** describes a design philosophy in which technology communicates what it needs to without demanding the user's full attention — operating at the periphery of awareness rather than the centre. A **calm technology** can be ignored when it does not need attention and can be brought to focus when it does.

**Origin:** The concept was developed by Mark Weiser and John Seely Brown at Xerox PARC in a 1995 essay *Designing Calm Technology*. Weiser, who had coined the term "ubiquitous computing," argued that as technology became more pervasive, the challenge would be designing it to recede — to inform without intruding. The concept was developed further by Amber Case, whose 2015 book *Calm Technology* updated it for the smartphone era.

> Design that places technology at the periphery of attention — informing without demanding focus, present without being intrusive.

**The Appeal:** **Calm technology** describes what most people actually want from their devices most of the time: to be informed, not interrupted. The concept offers a coherent design vocabulary for the opposite of what attention-economy platforms deliver — and has influenced thinking in ambient computing, wearables, and smart home design.

**The Friction:** **Calm technology** runs directly against the incentive structures of platform capitalism. [[Attention Economy]] — attention as scarce resource and commodity — makes **calm technology** commercially irrational: a product that recedes and informs without demanding engagement generates less data, less advertising revenue, and less platform dependency than one that maximises notifications and time-on-platform. [[Dopamine Feedback Loops]] — variable reward schedules — are the design mechanism that **calm technology**'s philosophy opposes. The 1995 vision of ubiquitous computing receding into the background was written before the attention economy existed. The devices became ubiquitous. They did not become calm.

**Why This Matters:** **Calm technology** names the road not taken — the design philosophy that describes what technology could be rather than what the **attention economy** makes it. Once you know the concept, every unnecessary notification is a failure of design, not an inevitability.
