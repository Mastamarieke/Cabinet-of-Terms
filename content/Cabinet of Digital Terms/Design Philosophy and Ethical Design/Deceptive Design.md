# Deceptive Design


***"Dark patterns" sounds like a design critique. "Deceptive design" sounds like a legal charge. The EU chose the second one deliberately.***

**Literal meaning:** **Deceptive design** describes interface and product design choices that mislead users — creating false impressions, hiding information, making unwanted actions easy and desired actions difficult, or exploiting cognitive biases to override users' stated preferences. The term is used in academic research and legal contexts in preference to the more colloquial "**dark patterns**."

**Origin:** "**Deceptive Design**" (sometimes referred to as “**dark patterns**”)was coined by UX designer **Harry Brignull** in 2010. In 2024 Researchers including **Colin Gray** et al. developed an Ontology of Dark Paterns. Mathur et al  discovered 1,818 **dark patterns** that were present on 1,254 of the ∼11K shopping websites.
The EU's Digital Services Act (2022) and the FTC in the United States began using **deceptive design** as a regulatory category. The European Data Protection Board issued guidelines on **deceptive design** in 2022. The shift from "**dark patterns**" to "**deceptive design**" matters: it moves the frame from aesthetics to law.

> The legally preferred term for interface design that misleads users — chosen over **dark patterns** because it assigns responsibility and enables enforcement.

**The Appeal:** The academic and legal precision of "**deceptive design**" enables regulatory action that "**dark patterns**" — a designer's colloquial term — does not. Once **deceptive design** is a legal category, companies can be fined, not just criticised. The EU has begun doing exactly that: EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR )
enforcement has produced multi-million euro fines for cookie consent designs that made refusal significantly harder than acceptance.

**The Friction:** The legal category is only as effective as enforcement capacity. [[Dark Patterns]] — the colloquial vocabulary — documents the practices; **deceptive design** frames them as actionable. But [[Vendor Lock-in]] — private companies managing public infrastructure — means that the most egregious **deceptive design** is often deployed in contexts where regulatory reach is weakest. [[Nudging]] — behaviour steering through environmental design — occupies the legally ambiguous zone between **deceptive design** and legitimate persuasion: the line between informing and manipulating is contested, and platforms exploit the ambiguity. [[Fair Patterns]] is the positive formulation: design that achieves the same outcomes through honest means.

**Why This Matters:** **Deceptive design** makes the vocabulary shift analytically significant. The same interface can be described as a dark pattern (a design criticism), a **deceptive design** (a legal category), or a business feature (a commercial decision). Which frame applies determines what consequences follow.
