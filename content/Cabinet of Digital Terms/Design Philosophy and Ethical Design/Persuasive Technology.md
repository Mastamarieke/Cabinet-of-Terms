# Persuasive Technology


***The app does not force you to keep scrolling. It simply makes stopping feel like the unnatural option.***

**Literal meaning:** **Persuasive technology** refers to interactive systems designed specifically to change human attitudes or behaviour — not through force or argument, but through the structure of the interface itself. The technology does the persuading; the user is unaware it is happening.

**Origin:** The term was coined by B.J. **Fogg** at Stanford's **Persuasive Technology** Lab in the late 1990s, formalised in his book *Persuasive Technology: Using Computers to Change What We Think and Do* (2003). **Fogg**'s "Behavior Model" identified triggers, ability, and motivation as the three levers of behavioural change — a framework subsequently adopted extensively by social media platforms, health apps, and commercial interfaces.

> Technology designed to change user behaviour or attitudes through structural interface choices, without argument or coercion — and often without the user's awareness.

**The Appeal:** **Fogg**'s original framework was applied to genuinely beneficial goals: encouraging physical activity, supporting medication adherence, reducing energy consumption. Persuasion by design can make healthy behaviours easier and harmful ones harder. When aligned with user interests, it is [[Frictionful Design]]'s counterpart: friction that serves the person using the system.

**The Friction:** The same mechanisms that can encourage exercise also power [[Dopamine Feedback Loops]] in social media — variable rewards, streaks, social comparison, notification pressure. [[Dark Patterns]] are **persuasive technology** deployed against user interests. The structural tools are identical; the direction of benefit differs. **Persuasive technology** gave platform designers both the vocabulary and the ethical permission to build systems optimised for engagement — regardless of what that engagement produces in users.

**Why This Matters:** **Persuasive technology** names the design intent that platforms rarely disclose. Knowing that an interface was built to change your behaviour — not to serve your goals — reframes every engagement signal. The notification you just responded to was not spontaneous. It was designed.
