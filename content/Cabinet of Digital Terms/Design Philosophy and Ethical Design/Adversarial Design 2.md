# *Adversarial Design*


***The poster does not explain the problem. It makes you feel the problem — and asks who benefits from it staying that way.***

**Literal meaning:** "Adversarial" means positioned against something or someone. **Adversarial design** uses design methods — objects, interfaces, spaces, images — to make power structures and political conflicts visible and felt, rather than smoothed over. It does not offer a solution. It creates confrontation.

**Origin:** The term was developed by **Carl DiSalvo** in *Adversarial Design* (MIT Press, 2012), drawing on political theorist **Chantal Mouffe**'s concept of agonism — the idea that democratic life requires genuine conflict, not consensus. DiSalvo argued that design could function as a political practice by making disagreement productive rather than resolved.

> Design that exposes power structures and makes political conflict legible, without resolving it into comfortable agreement.

**The Appeal:** Most design disciplines frame conflict as a problem to be solved. **Adversarial design** treats it as a condition to be made visible. For designers who work in civic, activist, or critical contexts, this offers a vocabulary for interventions that do not pretend neutrality. A piece of **adversarial design** can ask: who decided this? Who does it serve? Who is harmed?

**The Friction:** **Adversarial design** operates at the edge of design's institutional comfort zone. It does not produce usable products or satisfied clients. It can be absorbed by the same platforms it critiques — displayed as provocative art, defused as commentary. [[Deceptive Design]] continues while adversarial critiques of it circulate as conference papers. The gap between the critical gesture and structural change is the unresolved problem this term leaves open.

**Why This Matters:** Once you understand **adversarial design**, you can ask a different question about any designed object: is this thing making a conflict visible, or is it hiding one? A platform's clean interface may be **adversarial design**'s opposite — it smooths over the extraction it performs.
