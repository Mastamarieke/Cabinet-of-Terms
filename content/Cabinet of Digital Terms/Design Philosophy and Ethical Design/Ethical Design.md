***Design that starts with the question: what does this do to the people who use it?***

**Literal meaning:** **Ethical design** describes the practice of making design decisions — in interaction design, UX, product development, and digital services — that actively prioritise user wellbeing, autonomy, and dignity over metrics of engagement, conversion, or extraction. It encompasses transparency, **accessibility**, fairness, and the deliberate refusal to deploy manipulative or deceptive interface patterns.

**Origin:** The ethical critique of digital design has developed through several overlapping traditions in design ethics and interaction design. Mike Monteiro argues in _Ruined by Design_ (2019) that designers carry direct moral responsibility for the systems they create, rejecting the idea that design is a neutral or purely technical activity. Instead of merely executing business requirements, designers function as decision-makers who must question whether a product or feature should exist at all before determining how to implement it.

More recent work expands this critique from individual responsibility to systemic practice. In _Guiding the Future of Ethical Design_ (2024), M. Buckley describes how commercial incentives, platform economics, and organisational pressures often undermine ethical intentions in UX practice. **Ethical design** therefore requires not only personal accountability but also structural changes in how digital products are conceived, evaluated, and governed.

Within interaction design research, James Postell identifies several core ethical dimensions in _Designing Ethically in a Complex World_ (2024), including **accessibility**, adaptability, and conviviality. These principles emphasise designing for diverse and disabled users, reducing the cognitive burden created by technological systems, and supporting meaningful social interaction rather than isolation. Together, these perspectives frame ethical interaction design as an ongoing negotiation between user wellbeing, organisational power, and technological systems.

> A design practice that treats user wellbeing, autonomy, and dignity as non-negotiable requirements — not as features to be added after launch.

**The Appeal:** **Ethical design** makes a commercially coherent case alongside its moral one. Products that respect users build sustainable trust. Regulatory risk from [[Dark Patterns]] is documented and increasing — GDPR, the DSA, and the FTC have all moved toward enforcement. Brand damage from manipulative design is expensive. The business argument for **ethical design** is not idealism; it is a different time horizon.

**The Friction:** The term's widespread adoption is also its analytical problem. "**Ethical design**" has become a marketing claim deployed by companies whose products are not meaningfully constrained by ethics. This is the design equivalent of [[Privacy Washing]]: the vocabulary of ethics applied as a surface rather than as a genuine limit on product decisions. The distinction between **ethical design** practice — where user interests set actual constraints on what gets built — and **ethical design** rhetoric — where the language is adopted without the constraints — is rarely visible in product communications or procurement processes.

Monteiro's structural observation remains the sharpest: designers who work within organisations optimised for engagement, growth, and extraction face incentive structures that are hostile to ethical practice. [[VSD (Value Sensitive Design)]] and [[Fair Patterns]] provide methodological tools; they do not change the employment relationship. Postell's framework identifies what ethical dimensions look like in practice across interaction design's areas of concentration — but the framework's application depends on organisational conditions that most designers do not control.

**Why This Matters:** **Ethical design** names a practice and a standard. Once you have both the positive definition (**accessibility**, adaptability, conviviality, transparency, user autonomy) and the negative one (the absence of **dark patterns**, deceptive flows, and manipulative architecture), you can evaluate any product on both axes. The question is not "does this company claim to practice **ethical design**?" but "does this product's design impose ethical constraints that cost the company something?"
