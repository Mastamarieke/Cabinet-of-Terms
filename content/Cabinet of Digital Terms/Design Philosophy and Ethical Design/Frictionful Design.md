# Frictionful Design


***You want to delete your account. The platform asks: are you sure? Are you really sure? Have you considered what you'll lose?***

**Literal meaning:** Friction, in design, refers to anything that slows a user down — an extra step, a confirmation screen, a pause before an action completes. **Frictionful design** introduces this friction deliberately and productively: not to obstruct, but to create a moment of awareness before a consequential action.

**Origin:** The concept developed in HCI research as a counter-argument to the dominant "seamless" design ideal. Lars Hallnäs and Johan Redström introduced "slow technology" as early as 2001, arguing that efficiency is not always the appropriate design goal. **Colin Gray** and colleagues formalised "design frictions" in CHI 2016, distinguishing productive friction — which supports reflection — from dark pattern friction, which weaponises obstacles against users.

> Design that deliberately introduces moments of pause or resistance to prompt conscious decision-making before consequential actions.

**The Appeal:** Most digital systems are engineered to reduce friction to zero — to make the next click, purchase, or share feel effortless. **Frictionful design** operates on a different premise: that some decisions should require effort. A moment of resistance before posting, purchasing, or deleting creates the conditions for a choice that is actually chosen. This is not paternalism — it is restoring agency that seamless design removes.

**The Friction:** The same structural tool — added steps, confirmation screens, delays — is used by [[Dark Patterns]] to obstruct users who try to cancel subscriptions or access privacy settings. **Frictionful design** and dark patterns can look identical at the interface level. The difference lies in intent and direction: one slows you down for your benefit, the other for the platform's. That distinction is rarely visible to the user in the moment.

**Why This Matters:** **Frictionful design** forces a question about smoothness: whose interests does frictionless design serve? Once you notice that platforms make harmful actions easy and protective actions hard, the political economy of friction becomes readable. Every streamlined interface is a policy decision.
