# *Design Justice*


***The app was designed for everyone. Everyone who looks like the design team.***

**Literal meaning:** Justice, in design contexts, refers not to legal outcomes but to the equitable distribution of design benefits and harms. **Design justice** asks: who benefits from this design, who bears its costs, and — crucially — who was in the room when decisions were made?

**Origin:** The term was developed by Sasha Costanza-Chock in **Design Justice*: Community-Led Practices to Build the Worlds We Need* (MIT Press, 2020), emerging from the *Design Justice* Network, a coalition of practitioners, activists, and researchers founded around 2015. The framework draws on intersectionality theory — the understanding that systems of oppression (race, gender, class, disability) overlap and compound — and applies it directly to design practice.

> A framework that centres the people most harmed by design decisions as the primary agents of the design process, not its afterthought.

**The Appeal:** **Design justice** provides designers with a structural framework that goes beyond **accessibility** checklists or diversity hiring. It asks systemic questions: why does facial recognition software perform worse on darker skin tones? Not because engineers are malicious, but because training datasets reflected who was in the room. Centring marginalised communities in design processes produces systems that work better for more people.

**The Friction:** **Design justice** requires redistributing power, not just consulting users. [[Participatory Design]] — which involves users in the process — still tends to position designers as the final decision-makers. **Design justice** challenges that arrangement structurally. This makes it uncomfortable for institutions that fund design projects, and difficult to implement within standard commercial timelines and client relationships.

**Why This Matters:** **Design justice** makes the default visible. Every interface has a default user — and that default is rarely unmarked. Once you see it, the question shifts from "is this accessible?" to "who built this for whom, and who paid the cost of that choice?"
