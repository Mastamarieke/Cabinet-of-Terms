# VSD (Value Sensitive Design)


***Before building the system, someone asked: whose values does this embed — and who did not get a seat at the table?***

**Literal meaning:** Value Sensitive Design is a methodological framework for designing technology systems that explicitly accounts for human values — privacy, autonomy, informed consent, wellbeing, fairness — throughout the entire design process, not as a compliance step at the end.

**Origin:** VSD was developed by Batya Friedman at the University of Washington, formally articulated in her 1996 paper in *Human-Computer Interaction*. Friedman and colleagues including Helen Nissenbaum and Peter Kahn built a tripartite methodology: conceptual investigations (what values are at stake?), empirical investigations (how do people actually experience the system?), and technical investigations (how does the system support or undermine those values?). VSD has since been applied in domains from information systems to urban planning to autonomous vehicles.

> A design methodology that treats human values as requirements — identifying whose values, investigating trade-offs, and building them into the design from the start.

**The Appeal:** VSD gives designers a structured process for what they often want to do anyway: build things that are genuinely good for people. It translates ethical intention into methodological practice — moving from "we care about privacy" to "here is how we investigate and design for privacy systematically." It has generated a substantial body of applied research and is taught in design and computer science programmes worldwide.

**The Friction:** The methodology is only as strong as the institutional conditions that allow it. [[Dark Patterns]] — interface design that misleads users — are not design failures; they are design successes optimising for platform metrics rather than user values. VSD requires designers to have the authority to prioritise user values over engagement metrics — an authority that is structurally constrained in most commercial technology organisations. [[Predatory Design]] is what you get when VSD's questions are asked and then overridden by business requirements. [[Fair Patterns]] is VSD applied at the level of specific interface choices: the positive form of the same methodology. The gap between VSD as a framework and VSD as practice is the gap between a good intention and a structural condition.

**Why This Matters:** VSD makes visible that **ethical design** is not a technical problem — it is a political one. The question is not whether designers know how to design for human values. It is whether they are allowed to.
