# Seasteading


***Build a new society in international waters, outside any jurisdiction. The design is still in progress.***

**Literal meaning:** **Seasteading** is the concept of establishing permanent human settlements on floating platforms in international waters — outside any nation's jurisdiction, and therefore outside any nation's regulatory, tax, or legal frameworks. The term was coined by Patri Friedman (grandson of Milton Friedman) and developed by the **Seasteading** Institute, which received initial funding from Peter Thiel.

**Origin:** The **Seasteading** Institute was founded in 2008 by Patri Friedman and Peter Thiel, who provided $500,000 in initial funding. The founding idea: if you can choose which country to live in, you can exit bad governance; if you can build new governance structures from scratch in international waters, you can design optimal societies. French Polynesia entered negotiations with the Institute in 2017 for a pilot project, but the agreement collapsed in 2018. As of 2026, no operational seastead exists.

> The libertarian aspiration to escape governance by building new jurisdictions in international waters — still in the design phase.

**The Appeal:** **Seasteading** addresses a real frustration: that nation-states constrain innovation, that regulatory competition is limited by geography, and that people with radical ideas about governance have no place to try them. The argument for experimental governance — that variety allows for learning — has genuine merit in political philosophy.

**The Friction:** The gap between the aspiration and the achievement is analytically informative. After 18 years and substantial investment, no seastead exists — because the engineering, legal, economic, and political problems are vastly harder than the founding vision acknowledged. [[Fortress Futurism]] is the broader category: exit from shared social conditions as an elite project. [[Sovereignty as a Service]] is the adjacent concept: outsourcing governance to private infrastructure. [[Objectivism (Rand)]] provides the philosophical substrate: the state as the primary obstacle to human excellence. The people who fund **seasteading** are people for whom existing governance is primarily a constraint rather than a protection — which is a function of their position within it.

**Why This Matters:** **Seasteading** makes visible the political philosophy of exit: the idea that the appropriate response to bad governance is to leave rather than to improve it. Once you see that move, you can ask: for whom is exit available, and who remains in the governance they cannot escape?
