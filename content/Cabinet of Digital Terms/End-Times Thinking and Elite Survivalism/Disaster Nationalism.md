# Disaster Nationalism


***The crisis is an opportunity — for the right nation, led by the right people.***

**Literal meaning:** **Disaster nationalism** describes the political strategy of using genuine crises — pandemics, climate events, economic shocks, security incidents — to consolidate nationalist power, close borders, suppress opposition, and reframe structural political problems as threats requiring emergency nationalist response.

**Origin:** The concept builds on Naomi Klein's "shock doctrine" framework — the use of crises to impose policies that would be rejected in normal conditions — applied specifically to nationalist political actors. Political scientists including Cas Mudde (on populist radical right movements) and Thomas Friedman have documented the pattern: crises that objectively require international cooperation are reframed by nationalist actors as requiring national withdrawal and centralised authority.

> The systematic exploitation of genuine crises to legitimate nationalist consolidation — where the disaster provides the justification for the response.

**The Appeal:** **Disaster nationalism** speaks to real anxieties. Crises do create genuine threats. National governments do have responsibilities to their citizens. The argument that international cooperation is insufficient or unreliable during acute crises has empirical support in some contexts. The emotional appeal of nationalist protection during genuine danger is real.

**The Friction:** [[Millenarianism]] is the religious-ideological structure that **disaster nationalism** borrows: the corrupt present, the catastrophic transition, the chosen community that must be protected from outside contamination. [[Armageddon Complex]] is the adjacent concept: apocalyptic belief that justifies emergency power accumulation. [[Endtime Fascism]] is the more extreme form. The key analytical move of **disaster nationalism** is the reframing of collective problems (climate change, pandemic, economic instability) as threats from outside the nation rather than structural conditions requiring structural response. The emergency measures — border closures, surveillance expansion, opposition suppression — consistently outlast the emergency that justified them.

**Why This Matters:** **Disaster nationalism** makes visible the political use of crisis as resource. Once you see the reframing — shared problem becomes external threat — you can ask of any nationalist crisis response: what structural condition is being obscured by the emergency frame, and who benefits from that obscuration?
