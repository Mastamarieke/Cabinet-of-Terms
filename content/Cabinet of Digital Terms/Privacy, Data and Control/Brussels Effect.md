***The EU passed a law. A company in California changed its global product.***

**Literal meaning:** The **Brussels Effect** describes the mechanism by which EU regulation becomes the effective global standard for a sector — not through international treaty or diplomatic pressure but through the commercial logic of market access: companies that want to operate in the EU market must comply with EU rules, and compliance is often more economical as a single global standard than as a market-by-market variation.

**Origin:** The concept was developed and named by Columbia Law School professor Anu Bradford in a 2012 *Columbia Law Review* article and expanded in her book *The Brussels Effect* (2020). Bradford documented that EU regulations on privacy (GDPR), food safety, chemicals (REACH), and digital markets had become de facto global standards — not because other governments adopted them but because the global market leaders adjusted their products to EU requirements and extended those adjustments globally.

> The mechanism by which EU regulatory standards spread globally — because companies find it cheaper to apply one standard everywhere than to maintain separate versions.

**The Appeal:** The **Brussels Effect** demonstrates that effective regulation of global technology companies is possible from a single jurisdiction with sufficient market power. GDPR's data protection requirements, the Digital Services Act's content moderation obligations, and the AI Act's risk-based framework all have global reach through this mechanism. For advocates of technology governance, it is the most practical available path.

**The Friction:** The **Brussels Effect** is not a complete solution. [[CLOUD Act]] creates a direct conflict: US law requires US cloud providers to provide data to US authorities on request, regardless of where the data is stored — which conflicts with GDPR's data protection requirements. The conflict creates a structural tension in any EU organisation using US cloud infrastructure. [[Vendor Lock-in]] is compounded by the regulatory complexity: organisations that have built their infrastructure on US cloud platforms face both technical and regulatory lock-in. The **Brussels Effect** sets floors but does not address power asymmetries in the markets it regulates.

**Why This Matters:** **Brussels Effect** names the most consequential mechanism of global technology governance currently in operation. Once you see how it works — EU market access as regulatory lever — you can read EU digital regulation as having global scope, and assess its adequacy accordingly.
