# "We have never and will never sell user data."


***Technically true. Designed to be understood as meaning something it does not say.***

**The statement:** *"We have never and will never sell user data."* — **Mark Zuckerberg**, CEO of Meta, in congressional testimony and public statements, repeatedly from 2018 onward.

**The context:** The statement was made in multiple contexts — congressional hearings following the Cambridge Analytica revelations, public responses to privacy criticism, and media interviews. It became a repeated formulation, apparently settled and definitive.

**What the sentence does:** The sentence is technically accurate in a narrow sense: Meta does not hand raw user data to advertisers. What Meta sells is access to its advertising targeting system — the ability to reach audiences defined by extraordinarily detailed behavioural profiles, without the advertiser ever receiving the underlying data directly. The sentence is designed to be heard as "we protect your privacy" while meaning something considerably more limited: "we do not sell the file; we sell the key."

> A technically true sentence engineered to be understood as meaning something it does not say — where the narrow truth functions as a broad reassurance.

**The analytical work the sentence does:** It is a case study in what [[Privacy Washing]] — privacy rhetoric as marketing without corresponding practice — looks like when performed at the highest level of institutional communication. The statement exploits the gap between the legal/technical meaning of "sell user data" (transfer raw data files to third parties) and the ordinary meaning listeners assign to it (allow others to use information about me for profit). [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — human behaviour as raw material for prediction products — is what the statement is designed to obscure. [[Microtargeting]] is what the advertising system enables: not selling data, but selling the ability to reach people based on the data.

**Why this sentence matters:** Once you understand the gap between what "sell user data" technically means and what it communicates, you can apply the same analysis to any privacy statement. The precision is the deception. The sentence tells a true thing in a way that produces a false impression. That is a design choice.
