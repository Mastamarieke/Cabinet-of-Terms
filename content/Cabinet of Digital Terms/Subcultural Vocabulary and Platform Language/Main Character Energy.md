# Main Character Energy


***He narrates his commute as if someone is watching.***

**Literal meaning:** **Main Character Energy** (MCE) describes the persona or affect of someone who moves through the world as if they are the protagonist of a narrative — with the confidence, centrality, and narrative significance that protagonists have in fiction. Others are supporting characters, obstacles, or extras. The term is used both admiringly (to describe someone with compelling self-possession) and critically (to describe someone with an inflated sense of their own centrality).

**Origin:** The term emerged from **TikTok** in 2020–2021, popularised by videos in which creators framed mundane moments as cinematically significant — walking to music, responding to situations with protagonist-level composure. The critical usage — "you're being a main character right now" as a critique of someone's self-centredness — emerged simultaneously. Researchers studying parasocial media have documented the psychological framework; media scholar Robyn Wiegman and others have theorised the protagonist identification structure.

> A self-presentation mode that frames one's own experience as the primary narrative — and positions everyone else as supporting cast.

**The Appeal:** **Main character energy** is genuine empowerment when it means treating your own life as worth living with intention and self-possession. 
McAdams is useful for _Main Character Energy_ because he frames identity as narrative: people make sense of themselves by turning life into a story. In internet culture, _main character energy_ literalizes that idea — the self is imagined as protagonist, everyday life becomes scene-setting, and identity is performed through narrative framing.  In this sense, _main character energy_ can be empowering — a claim to agency and narrative control — but it also reflects the [[Influencer]]-era pressure to aestheticize the self for an imagined audience.

**The Friction:** Social media changes the structure. [[Attention Economy]] provides a literal audience: the protagonist's life is being watched, liked, and shared. [[Personal Branding]] is the professional form: you are the protagonist of your professional story. [[Aurafarming]] is the status management component. The critical dimension — "you're the main character" as a critique — identifies the specific harm: treating other people as supporting cast rather than protagonists of their own stories. [[Comparison Culture]] is intensified: in a media environment where everyone is performing protagonist status, the comparison between whose life looks more protagonist-worthy is continuous.

**Why This Matters:** **Main Character Energy** makes visible the specific self-positioning that social media rewards — and the specific cost of that positioning for how people relate to others. [[Situated Knowledge]] — **Haraway**'s insistence that all perspectives are located — is the epistemological corrective: everyone is the main character of their own story, which means no one is.
