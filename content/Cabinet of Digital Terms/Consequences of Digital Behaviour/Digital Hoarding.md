***Your downloads folder has 4,000 files. You have not opened most of them. You cannot delete them either.***

**Literal meaning**
*Digital* — existing in digital form. *Hoarding* — compulsive accumulation of objects beyond use. **Digital Hoarding** is the persistent accumulation of digital files, emails, screenshots, and media combined with an inability or unwillingness to delete them.

**Origin**
Nick Neave and colleagues at Northumbria University first documented **digital hoarding** as a distinct behavioural pattern in 2016, drawing on the clinical literature on hoarding disorder. Unlike physical hoarding, **digital hoarding** is enabled by near-unlimited cheap storage — there is no material pressure to delete.

> The compulsive accumulation of digital files combined with an inability to delete them — a disorder made easier by unlimited cheap storage.

**The Appeal**
Keeping files feels rational: storage is cheap, you might need it later, and deletion is irreversible. The logic of abundance makes accumulation the path of least resistance.

**The Friction**
Neave et al. (2018) document that **digital hoarding** produces anxiety, cognitive overload, and impaired information retrieval — the same consequences as physical hoarding, without the visible signal. Platform design accelerates it: unlimited cloud storage removes the friction that would otherwise trigger sorting and deletion. The disorder is invisible until it disrupts work or causes distress.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see **digital hoarding** as a design outcome rather than a personal failure, you see that the architecture of unlimited storage and easy accumulation produces the condition — and that the solution is not willpower but friction by design.
