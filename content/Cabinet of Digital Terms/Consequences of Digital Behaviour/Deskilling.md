# Deskilling

***The tool does it faster. You use the tool. One day the tool is unavailable.***

**Literal meaning**
*De-* — removal. *Skill* — a learned capacity requiring practice. **Deskilling** is the process by which reliance on tools or systems erodes the foundational skills that made those tools optional.

**Origin**
Harry Braverman introduced **deskilling** in *Labor and Monopoly Capital* (1974), documenting how Taylorist management separated conception from execution, degrading craft knowledge into routine tasks. Brynjolfsson and McAfee (2011) extended the analysis to digital automation, showing how algorithmic systems progressively replace cognitive skills — navigation, arithmetic, memory — that humans previously maintained through practice.

> The erosion of human capability through sustained reliance on automated systems — where convenience today becomes dependency tomorrow.

**The Appeal**
Offloading routine tasks to technology frees human attention for higher-order work. GPS, calculators, and spell-checkers are genuine productivity tools.

**The Friction**
The friction is cumulative and invisible. Each skill outsourced is initially optional — but over time the capacity atrophies. Braverman's insight, updated for the digital age: the system that replaces the skill also makes you dependent on it. [[AI Dependency]] is the contemporary expression: **cognitive offloading** to AI systems may be accelerating **deskilling** at scale, across domains from writing to reasoning.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see **deskilling** as structural rather than personal, you see that the question is not whether to use tools — it is which capacities are worth maintaining deliberately, against the grain of convenience.
