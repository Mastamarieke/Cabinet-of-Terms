# Manosphere


***The forums do not advertise themselves as ideology. They advertise themselves as self-improvement.***

**Literal meaning:** The **manosphere** is not a single community but a loose ecosystem of online spaces — forums, **YouTube** channels, podcasts, subreddits, **TikTok** accounts — united by anti-feminist perspectives and a shared vocabulary of male grievance and hierarchy. It includes pickup artists (PUA), men's rights activists (MRA), men going their own way (**MGTOW**), incels, and red-pill communities, among others.

**Origin:** The term was coined by blogger Marky Mark in 2009. The term _manosphere_ emerged online in the late 2000s and was later popularized by blogger Ian Ironwood, who used it to describe a loose network of websites, forums, and online communities centered around masculinity, dating, gender relations, and men’s issues. Academic research on the **manosphere** expanded significantly during the mid-2010s through the work of scholars such as Debbie Ging and Michael Kimmel, who examined how online spaces like **incel** forums, men’s rights activism, and “**red pill**” communities shape contemporary ideas about masculinity and antifeminism. Researchers have also explored how **manosphere** content spread from fringe internet forums to mainstream social media platforms through algorithmic amplification, **influencer** culture, and the **creator economy**.

Influencers commonly associated with the **manosphere** include Andrew Tate, who promotes traditional masculinity, wealth, dominance, and male authority; Jordan Peterson, known for emphasizing personal responsibility, discipline, and traditional social values; Myron Gaines, who discusses dating dynamics and “**red pill**” ideology; Rollo Tomassi, recognized for popularizing evolutionary and “**red pill**” perspectives on male–female relationships; Adin Ross, whose livestreams have featured **manosphere**-related discussions and personalities; and Hamza Ahmed, who focuses on male self-improvement, discipline, fitness, and productivity. Critics argue that parts of the **manosphere** promote misogyny, anti-feminist beliefs, and harmful stereotypes about women, while supporters often describe the movement as a form of guidance on masculinity, self-improvement, and modern male identity.

> A networked ecosystem of anti-feminist online communities — diverse in tone and tactics, unified by the claim that men are the primary victims of gender politics.

**The Appeal:** The **manosphere** addresses genuine male experiences: loneliness, romantic rejection, economic precarity, declining social status relative to previous generations. These experiences are real. The communities provide belonging, vocabulary, and explanation. For isolated young men, the sense of having found others who understand is powerful and not trivial.

**The Friction:** The explanation offered is consistently the same: women, feminism, and progressive institutions are responsible. [[Recommender Systems]] — algorithms that surface increasingly engaging content — have been documented as radicalisation vectors: a fitness video leads to a **red pill** video leads to an incel forum. Researcher Robyn Caplan at Data & Society documented the pipeline. [[SMV (Sexual Market Value)]] is the shared framework that connects the communities: attractiveness as a market determines outcomes, and the market is rigged. [[Incel]] is one terminus of the logic; [[Blackpill]] is another. The BBC documentary series on the **manosphere** and the documentary *The Brainwashing of My Dad* document how the pipeline operates at family level.

**Why This Matters:** **Manosphere** names an ecosystem, not a monolith. Once you see it as an ecosystem — with entry points, graduation pathways, and community structures — "he watches those videos" becomes a different kind of observation.
