# Sharenting


***The photo was posted before the child could speak. By the time they are old enough to object, there are thousands.***

**Literal meaning:** **Sharenting** — a portmanteau of *sharing* and *parenting* — describes the practice of parents sharing images, videos, and personal information about their children on social media. The term covers a spectrum from occasional family photos to systematic documentation of a child's life as content.

**Origin:** The term was coined by journalist Steven Leckart in a 2012 *Wall Street Journal* article and entered broader use as smartphone photography made constant documentation and sharing frictionless. Legal scholar Stacey Steinberg produced foundational academic work on **sharenting** and children's digital privacy rights, arguing in a 2017 *University of Florida Law Review* article that children have a right to informational self-determination that **sharenting** may compromise.

> The construction of a child's digital identity by a parent before the child can consent — producing a permanent public record the child did not choose.

**The Appeal:** Sharing children's moments is a normal expression of parental love and a way to maintain connection with family and friends at distance. The impulse is genuine, the technology is frictionless, and the social rewards — likes, congratulations, engagement — are real. For many parents, **sharenting** is simply what parenting in a connected world looks like.

**The Friction:** The child cannot consent. The digital footprint created in childhood is permanent and may follow the child into adulthood: searchable, screenshottable, potentially embarrassing or harmful. [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — human behaviour as raw material — means that images of children feed algorithmic training data, advertising systems, and facial recognition databases. [[Momfluencer]] and [[Family Vlogging]] are the commercial intensification: **sharenting** at scale, as business model. Steinberg's research documented that the average parent shares roughly 1,000 images of a child online before their fifth birthday.

**Why This Matters:** **Sharenting** makes visible who owns a child's digital identity before they are old enough to have an opinion about it. Once you see the data infrastructure the images enter, the decision to post looks different.
