# Predatory Design


***The game is free. The rewards come quickly at first, then slow down. Speeding them up costs money. The target audience is eight years old.***

**Literal meaning:** **Predatory design** describes interface and product design choices that deliberately exploit the specific developmental vulnerabilities of children — weaker impulse control, greater susceptibility to variable rewards, incomplete understanding of monetary value, and stronger peer pressure sensitivity — to maximise engagement, spending, and data disclosure.

**Origin:** The term developed alongside child online safety research and regulation from the early 2010s. The UK Age Appropriate Design Code (Children's Code, 2021), developed by the Information Commissioner's Office under the direction of Baroness Beeban Kidron of 5Rights Foundation, became the primary regulatory framework: 15 standards requiring platforms to build child safety in by default when children are likely users.

> Interface design that exploits children's developmental vulnerabilities — using the same manipulation techniques as adult platforms on people who cannot yet recognise them.

**The Appeal:** From a commercial perspective, children are an attractive user population: they influence household spending, form brand loyalties early, and have less capacity to evaluate commercial intent. Many platforms used primarily by adults are also heavily used by children, and most were designed without any consideration of developmental appropriateness.

**The Friction:** [[Dark Patterns]] applied to adults exploit cognitive biases. Applied to children, the same techniques exploit developmental stages: [[Loot Boxes]] exploit the developing reward-learning system; [[Robux]] exploits the developing understanding of monetary value; notification systems exploit the developing capacity for impulse control. [[Gaming Disorder]] is one documented clinical consequence. [[Roblox]] is a prominent example: designed as a gaming platform, deployed to children as young as four, with social features that create [[Online Grooming]] risk alongside the gaming risk. The UK Children's Code has been the most effective regulatory response; platforms including **TikTok**, **Instagram**, and **YouTube** were found non-compliant and required to change designs.

**Why This Matters:** **Predatory design** names the deliberate exploitation of developmental vulnerability as commercial strategy. Once you see it, "it's just a game" and "the child just needs more self-control" become inadequate responses to a designed condition.
