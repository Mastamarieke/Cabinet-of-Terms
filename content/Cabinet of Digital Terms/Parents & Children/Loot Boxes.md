***You pay for a chance. Sometimes you get what you wanted. Sometimes you do not. You can pay again.***

**Literal meaning:** **Loot boxes** are purchasable virtual containers in games that provide randomised in-game rewards — items, characters, cosmetics, or in-game advantages. The player pays a fixed price for a probabilistic outcome, which may or may not be what they wanted.

**Origin:** The mechanism was introduced into mainstream gaming from around 2007 (Valve's *Team Fortress 2*) and scaled rapidly from 2012–2016 as mobile gaming and free-to-play models monetised through in-app purchases. The EA game *FIFA* (now *EA Sports FC*) made **loot boxes** a central controversy: the Ultimate Team mode, in which players purchase card packs for randomised player cards, generated billions in annual revenue.

> A probabilistic purchase mechanism in gaming — structurally identical to gambling, deployed to children in markets where gambling regulations do not apply.

**The Appeal:** **Loot boxes** are engaging. The anticipation of the random reward — the "unboxing" moment — is genuinely pleasurable and shares its neurological mechanism with other variable reward experiences. For game developers, **loot boxes** are enormously profitable: a small percentage of high-spending players ("whales") generate a disproportionate share of revenue.

**The Friction:** The structural identity with gambling is the analytical core. Both involve paying for a probabilistic outcome, both exploit variable ratio reinforcement schedules, and both produce documented problem behaviour in susceptible individuals. [[Predatory Design]] applied to children is the context: adolescents are developmentally more susceptible to gambling-like reward mechanisms than adults, and **loot boxes** are deployed without age restriction. Research by Zendle and Cairns (2019) in *PLOS ONE* documented a significant correlation between loot box spending and problem gambling scores. Belgium and the Netherlands banned **loot boxes** as gambling in 2018. The UK Gambling Commission's 2023 review found insufficient evidence for a blanket ban but recommended tighter regulation.

**Why This Matters:** **Loot boxes** make visible how gambling mechanics can be embedded in children's products under a different name. Once you see the structural identity, the regulatory question — is this gambling? — is less important than the design question: why was this mechanism deployed to children?
