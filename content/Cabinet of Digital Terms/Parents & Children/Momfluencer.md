# Momfluencer


***The child is in every video. The child is also the product.***

**Literal meaning:** A **momfluencer** is a mother who builds a social media audience around parenting content — using her children, home, and family life as the subject matter of a content business, monetised through brand partnerships, sponsored content, and affiliate marketing.

**Origin:** The term emerged from the broader **influencer** economy in the mid-2010s, as **Instagram** and later **TikTok** enabled mothers to build large audiences around parenting content. The category scaled rapidly: by 2020, "mommy blogger" had evolved into "**momfluencer**" and the category represented a significant advertising market. Researcher Brooke Erin Duffy’s work on aspirational labour in digital media provides the academic framework for understanding **influencer** culture as a form of precarious, often unpaid self-branding work; journalist Stephanie McNeal’s _Swipe Up for More!: Inside the Unfiltered Lives of Influencers_ (2023) explores how influencers shape consumption, identity, and online attention economies in everyday life.

> A content business in which a mother monetises family life — with the child as the product's protagonist and primary draw.

**The Appeal:** **Momfluencer** content addresses genuine parenting questions, provides community for isolated parents, and normalises the real complexity of family life. For creators, the business model enables income while spending time with children. The most popular momfluencers are genuinely entertaining, informative, and relatable.

**The Friction:** The child is the product's protagonist without capacity to consent. [[Sharenting]] is the personal practice; momfluencing is the commercial intensification. [[Family Vlogging]] is the video form. [[Kidfluencer]] is what happens when the child becomes the primary brand. Unlike adult influencers who choose their public exposure, the children in **momfluencer** content do not — and the content becomes a permanent part of their digital record. Research by Livingstone and colleagues at LSE has documented children's own views of parental sharing: many express discomfort with content posted without their knowledge.

**Why This Matters:** **Momfluencer** makes visible the specific commercial structure in which a child's childhood becomes a content asset. The mother's labour is real; the child's exposure is not chosen. Once you see the business model, the family photo is also a product shot.
