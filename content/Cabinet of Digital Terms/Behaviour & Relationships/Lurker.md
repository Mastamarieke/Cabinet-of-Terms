# Lurker


***Present but invisible. Reading without responding.***

**Literal meaning:** A **lurker** is someone who reads, watches, or observes online content and communities without posting, commenting, or otherwise making their presence known. The term predates social media — it was used in early internet forums and bulletin boards from the 1980s — but the behaviour it describes has become the dominant mode of online participation.

**Origin:** The term comes from early internet culture, where forum moderators and participants used it to describe users who consumed content without contributing. Early online communities sometimes had explicit norms around lurking — asking newcomers to "lurk more" before posting, or treating prolonged lurking as suspicious. Research on online communities from the 1990s onward documented that the majority of members in most communities were lurkers — a pattern confirmed repeatedly across platforms and eras.

> The dominant mode of online participation — consuming content without contributing, present without being visible.

**The Appeal:** Lurking is a legitimate and often rational choice. Many platforms are not safe spaces for everyone. The costs of visibility — harassment, misreading, permanent record — are real. Lurking allows participation in information and community without those risks. For marginalised users in particular, lurking may be the only viable form of presence.

**The Friction:** At scale, lurking shapes the information environment in ways individual lurkers do not intend. [[Attention Economy]] — attention as scarce resource and commodity — is fed by lurkers: the view, the watch time, the dwell time all register as engagement data even without active participation. The platform profits from the **lurker**'s presence without the **lurker**'s knowledge that they are contributing. [[Recommender Systems]] are trained partly on lurking behaviour — what you watched, what you did not skip, how long you hovered — creating a detailed profile from apparent absence. [[Phatic Interaction]] is what platforms push lurkers toward: the low-friction like that converts invisible presence into trackable signal.

**Why This Matters:** Lurking makes visible the asymmetry between user experience and platform reality. You are not just watching. You are being watched watching.
