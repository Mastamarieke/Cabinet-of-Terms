***Your phone is not vibrating. You feel it anyway.***

**Literal meaning:** **Phantom vibration syndrome** describes the experience of perceiving your phone vibrating when it is not. The sensation is real — felt in the leg, pocket, or hand — but has no external source. The nervous system has learned to anticipate the notification and begins generating the signal independently.

**Origin:** The phenomenon was first documented formally by researcher Robert Rosenberger in a 2013 paper, though anecdotal accounts appeared in tech journalism from the mid-2000s. The experience is extremely common: studies have found it in 68–90% of smartphone users. Rosenberger described it using philosopher Don Ihde's framework of technological embodiment — the phone becomes incorporated into the body schema, like a tool used so habitually it is felt as an extension of the self.

> The sensation of phone vibration when the phone is not vibrating — a physical symptom of habitual smartphone dependency.

**The Appeal:** **Phantom vibration syndrome** is not harmful in itself. It is a byproduct of a device people use heavily and value — a misfiring of the same attentional system that makes the phone useful. Most people who experience it find it mildly amusing rather than distressing.

**The Friction:** The phenomenon makes visible something that is otherwise invisible: the degree to which the device has been incorporated into the body's anticipatory system. [[Dopamine Feedback Loops]] — variable reward schedules that maintain engagement — are the mechanism that makes the phone worth anticipating. The body learns to expect the reward and begins generating the expectation independently. [[Nomophobia]] — anxiety produced by device absence — shares the same substrate: the phone has become part of the nervous system's baseline. [[Technostress]] is the broader category; **phantom vibration syndrome** is its most literal, most physical expression.

**Why This Matters:** **Phantom vibration syndrome** is the clearest evidence that the phone is not just a tool you carry — it has become part of how your body experiences the world. Once you know that, the question of what you are giving it access to becomes a different kind of question.
