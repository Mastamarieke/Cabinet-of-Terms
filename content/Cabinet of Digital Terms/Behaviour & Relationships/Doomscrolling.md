# Doomscrolling


***You know the news is bad. You keep scrolling.***

**Literal meaning:** **Doomscrolling** describes the compulsive consumption of negative, distressing, or anxiety-inducing news and content through continuous scrolling — particularly on social media and news platforms. The user typically knows the content is harmful to their mental state and continues anyway.

**Origin:** The term circulated in tech and media discourse from around 2018 and gained widespread use during the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, when a convergence of crisis news, lockdown conditions, and increased smartphone use produced documented increases in compulsive news consumption. Merriam-Webster added it to the dictionary in 2020. The behaviour predates the term — but the term named something people immediately recognised.

> Compulsive consumption of negative news despite awareness of its psychological cost — a behaviour that platform design makes easier than stopping.

**The Appeal:** **Doomscrolling** is not irrational. During crises, staying informed is genuinely important. The drive to monitor threatening situations is a feature of human threat-detection systems — keeping watch is adaptive behaviour, and the platform delivers an endless supply of things to watch. The anxiety is appropriate to the situation; the compulsion is appropriate to the design.

**The Friction:** [[Recommender Systems]] — algorithms that determine what you see — optimise for engagement, and negative content generates more engagement than neutral content. The feed is not a neutral information source: it is an engagement-optimised environment in which distressing content is structurally amplified. [[Dopamine Feedback Loops]] — variable reward schedules — make the next scroll feel necessary: maybe the next item will resolve the anxiety rather than extend it. It rarely does. [[Cognitive Surrender]] is the downstream effect: the capacity for self-interruption weakens with sustained use. [[Technostress]] is the documented clinical outcome.

**Why This Matters:** **Doomscrolling** makes visible the gap between a behaviour and its cause. The scrolling feels like a personal failure of self-control. The design is what makes stopping harder than continuing.
