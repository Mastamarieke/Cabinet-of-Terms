# Greenwashing


***The sustainability pledge was published the same week as the data centre expansion announcement.***

**Literal meaning:** Presenting a company, product, or policy as environmentally responsible through communication — without making substantive changes to the practices causing environmental harm.

**Origin:** Environmentalist Jay Westerveld coined the term in 1986 in response to hotel towel-reuse campaigns — small sustainability gestures deployed to distract from far larger environmental impacts. From the early 2020s it became central to AI energy debate as major tech companies began publishing sustainability pledges alongside rapidly expanding infrastructure.

> Environmental language deployed as reputation management, without proportional change in actual practice.

**The Appeal:** Sustainability pledges serve a real function: they create public accountability, attract talent, and sometimes produce genuine efficiency gains. Carbon offset markets and renewable energy certificates are not always empty. Inside organisations, the sustainability team may be entirely sincere.

**The Friction:** The problem is structural. [[AI Energy Consumption]] is growing faster than renewable energy capacity can absorb it. A company can be simultaneously carbon neutral on paper — through offsets and certificates — and responsible for rapidly increasing absolute emissions. [[Externalized Costs]] — environmental burdens shifted onto communities rather than absorbed by the company — are not captured by carbon accounting. What is measured is chosen. What is not measured does not appear in the sustainability report.

**Why This Matters:** Once you know **greenwashing**, a press release is never only a press release. Pledges set in the future are not actions in the present. The gap between those two sentences is where scrutiny lives.
