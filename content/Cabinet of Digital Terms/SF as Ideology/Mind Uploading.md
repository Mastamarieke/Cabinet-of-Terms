# Mind Uploading


***Live forever in a server. Whether that is still you is a question for philosophers. The engineers have already moved on.***

**Literal meaning:** **Mind uploading** — also called whole brain emulation — describes the hypothetical process of scanning a brain at sufficient resolution to create a digital copy of its structure and function, enabling a person's consciousness to persist in a computational substrate independently of their biological body.

**Origin:** The concept has roots in SF from the 1950s–60s and was given philosophical and technical grounding by figures including Hans Moravec (*Mind Children*, 1988) and later Randal Koene (who leads the carboncopies.org project). It is a central aspiration of transhumanist philosophy and has been discussed by technologists including **Elon Musk** and Ray Kurzweil. The technical barriers are enormous: a complete wiring diagram of even a small mammal's brain remains beyond current capability; a human brain has approximately 86 billion neurons with trillions of connections.

> The aspiration to preserve consciousness digitally — where the technical feasibility is speculative, and the question of whether it would still be you is unanswered.

**The Appeal:** **Mind uploading** addresses the universal human fear of death. If consciousness could be digitally preserved, death becomes optional rather than inevitable. The aspiration is ancient in its emotional register. Its technological framing makes it feel achievable rather than mythological.

**The Friction:** The philosophical problem is fundamental. Even if a perfect digital copy of your brain could be created, the copy would not be continuous with you — it would be a new entity with your memories. The question of whether that constitutes survival of consciousness or creation of a replica is not resolved by the technology; it is a philosophical question the technology sidesteps. [[Transhumanism]] treats this as a technical problem awaiting a solution. Critics including philosopher David Chalmers have argued the "hard problem of consciousness" makes **mind uploading**'s identity claims incoherent. [[Neuralink]] and [[Brain-Computer Interface]] are the incremental technical steps in the same direction. The social dimension — **mind uploading** as a service for whom? — receives almost no attention in the discourse.

**Why This Matters:** **Mind uploading** makes visible how SF ideas enter serious discourse as aspirations rather than proposals — without the scrutiny that policy proposals require. Once you ask who would have access and at what cost, the universalist framing of "humanity transcending death" becomes legible as a class project.
