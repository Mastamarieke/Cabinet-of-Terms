# SF as Ideology


***Science fiction became the business plan. The singularity, Mars colonisation, mind uploading — these are not metaphors. They are funding pitches.***

This cluster maps science fiction terms deployed as serious policy or identity rhetoric. When the impossible becomes the inevitable, ideological choices hide behind visionary language.

The question this cluster asks: who benefits from making the future sound inevitable?

---

## Terms in this cluster

### [[Digital Frankenstein]]
Technology that outgrows its maker and cannot be recalled. The oldest warning in the genre — still running.

### [[The Singularity]]
The moment AI surpasses human intelligence. Kurzweil set a date. The date keeps moving.

### [[AGI]]
Artificial General Intelligence — the threshold where the technical and the science-fictional blur into each other.

### [[Transhumanism]]
The ideology that technology must transcend the human condition. Death as an engineering problem.

### [[Mind Uploading]]
Digitising consciousness. Living forever in a server. The question of whether that is still you goes unanswered.

### [[Simulation Hypothesis]]
Bostrom: we probably live in a computer simulation. A philosophical thought experiment that became a talking point for billionaires.

### [[The Matrix]]
Reference frame for red pill, woke, reality tunnels. A film about liberation, repurposed as recruitment material.

### [[Brave New World]]
Huxley's soft control through pleasure. More relevant to platforms than 1984 — and less often cited.

### [[1984]]
Shorthand for surveillance. Claimed simultaneously by every side of the political spectrum.

### [[Skynet]]
The AI that decided humanity was the threat. A movie villain that now appears in serious policy debates.

### [[Mars Colonization]]
Musk's SF utopia as policy frame. Escape from Earth as a private project.

### [[Cybernetic Collective]]
Human-machine fusion at the level of society. The collective framing obscures who owns the infrastructure.

### [[Neuralink]]
Brain-computer interface. The name itself is SF aesthetic — science fiction as brand identity.

### [[Brain-Computer Interface]]
Direct connection between brain and machine. The technology is real. The implications are still being written.

### [[Doomerism]]
The belief that technological disaster is inevitable and resistance is futile. Fatalism as a political position.

### [[Longtermism]]
MacAskill and Ord: the moral weight of the future vastly outweighs the present. A framework that can justify almost anything today.

### [[Mythos Moment]]
The threshold where a model becomes too dangerous to release. Where science fiction becomes policy reality.
