***We are probably living in a simulation. Bostrom calculated the odds. Musk brought it up at a party.***

**Literal meaning:** The **simulation hypothesis**, as formalised by philosopher Nick Bostrom in a 2003 paper, proposes a trilemma: either virtually all civilisations go extinct before reaching the computational capacity to run detailed simulations of their history; or virtually all civilisations with that capacity choose not to run such simulations; or we are almost certainly living in a simulation run by a post-human civilisation.

**Origin:** Bostrom published "Are You Living in a Computer Simulation?" in the *Philosophical Quarterly* in 2003. The argument is formally rigorous within its assumptions. It entered tech culture through celebrity endorsement: **Elon Musk** stated in 2016 that "the odds that we're in base reality is one in billions." The **simulation hypothesis** became a recurring conversation topic in Silicon Valley. It has genuine philosophical lineage connecting to Descartes' evil demon and Putnam's brain in a vat.

> A philosophical argument that we are probably living in a computer simulation — taken seriously enough in tech culture to appear in investment pitches.

**The Appeal:** The **simulation hypothesis** has genuine philosophical interest. It connects to deep questions about consciousness, reality, and the nature of existence. For technically minded people, the computational framing makes ancient philosophical questions newly tractable. It also has entertainment value: if the simulation is indistinguishable from base reality, the question is practically inert.

**The Friction:** The hypothesis does ideological work when deployed outside academic philosophy. If reality is a simulation, then present material conditions — inequality, suffering, climate change — are simulated, which softens the urgency of addressing them. [[The Matrix]] is the pop culture version: **red pill** epistemology as simulation escape. [[Longtermism]] is the adjacent framework: both orient moral weight away from present material conditions toward speculative future states. The billionaire fascination with the **simulation hypothesis** is worth examining sociologically: the idea that everything is constructed is considerably more appealing when you are materially comfortable than when you are not.

**Why This Matters:** **Simulation hypothesis** makes visible how philosophical speculations travel into ideological territory. The argument's formal validity is real; its social function is different. Once you see the social context of the discourse, "we might be in a simulation" sounds different from its speakers' mouths.
