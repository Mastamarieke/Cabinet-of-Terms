***Before a product launches, test subjects wear electrodes while watching the ad. Their brain responses — not their opinions — determine what gets changed.***

**Literal meaning:** **Neuromarketing** applies neuroscience and physiological measurement — brain imaging (fMRI, EEG), eye tracking, facial coding, galvanic skin response — to marketing and design problems, measuring subconscious responses to products, advertisements, and interfaces to inform design choices that bypass conscious evaluation.

**Origin:** The field emerged from consumer neuroscience research in the early 2000s. Harvard Business School professor Gerald Zaltman's work on subconscious consumer motivation (ZMET methodology, 2003) was an early influence. The **Neuromarketing** Science & Business Association was founded in 2012. Major companies including Google, Microsoft, and major FMCG brands have employed **neuromarketing** research. The methodologies increasingly intersect with biometric data collection in digital environments — where dwell time, scroll speed, and mouse movement function as proxies for the physiological signals measured in lab settings.

> The use of brain scans, eye tracking, and physiological measurement to design more persuasive products — optimising for responses you are not aware of having.

**The Appeal:** **Neuromarketing** addresses a genuine limitation of traditional research: people cannot accurately report their own subconscious responses. Physiological measurements are more reliable than self-reported preferences for certain design decisions. For designers, the data provides objective grounds for choices that would otherwise be subjective.

**The Friction:** The optimisation target is subconscious response — which means the user cannot evaluate, negotiate, or consent to what is being optimised for. [[Dark Patterns]] optimise for behaviour against explicit preference; **neuromarketing** optimises for behaviour below the level of explicit preference. [[Nudging]] uses choice architecture to steer; **neuromarketing** designs the architecture using physiological response data. [[Biometric Surveillance]] is the infrastructure overlap: the same sensors that detect emotional responses can identify individuals. [[Fair Patterns]] is the design alternative: using the same knowledge of human psychology to design for user benefit rather than against user agency.

**Why This Matters:** **Neuromarketing** makes visible the gap between what you consciously decide and what your body responds to. Once that gap is systematically measured and designed into interfaces, the question of whose interests are being optimised for becomes urgent.
