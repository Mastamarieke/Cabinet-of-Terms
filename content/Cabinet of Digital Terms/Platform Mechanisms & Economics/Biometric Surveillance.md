***The camera does not just see you. It measures you — your face, your pace, your pulse — and stores the result.***

**Literal meaning:** **Biometric surveillance** is the collection, processing, and analysis of physical or behavioural characteristics — facial features, fingerprints, iris patterns, gait, voice, heartrate — to identify, track, or make inferences about individuals. In the platform context, it includes facial recognition in consumer devices, emotion detection in advertising systems, and the use of physiological signals as engagement and targeting data.

> The collection and analysis of physical body signals — face, gait, heartrate — to identify, track, or make inferences about people.

**The Appeal:** Biometric authentication is genuinely convenient and secure. Face ID unlocks a phone faster and more securely than a PIN. Fitness trackers that monitor heartrate provide useful health data. The consumer applications are real and valued.

**The Friction:** The same technology that unlocks your phone can identify you in a public space without your knowledge. [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — human behaviour as raw material — extends to physical presence when biometric data is collected passively. [[Deadnaming]] is one specific consequence: facial recognition systems may identify a trans person by their former name, which is retained in databases they cannot access or correct. [[Algorithmic Violence]] — harm caused by automated systems without individual decisions — describes what happens when biometric systems make consequential errors: misidentification in criminal justice systems has led to wrongful arrests. The EU AI Act (2024) restricts real-time **biometric surveillance** in public spaces — a recognition that the technology requires specific governance.

**Why This Matters:** **Biometric surveillance** makes the body readable. Once your face is a data point, your presence in a space generates a record you did not choose to create. The body is no longer private by default.
