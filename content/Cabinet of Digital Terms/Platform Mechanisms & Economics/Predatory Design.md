***The game is free. The rewards come quickly at first, then slow down. Speeding them up costs money. The target audience is eight years old.***

**Literal meaning:** **Predatory design** describes interface and product design choices that deliberately exploit the specific vulnerabilities of children — developmental, psychological, and cognitive — to maximise engagement, spending, and data disclosure. It is **dark patterns** applied to a population that lacks the developmental capacity to recognise or resist manipulation.

**Origin:** The term developed alongside research on children's digital environments from the early 2010s. The UK Age Appropriate Design Code (Children's Code, 2021) — developed by the Information Commissioner's Office — became the primary regulatory framework, requiring platforms to apply higher design standards when children are likely users. California's Age-Appropriate Design Code Act (2022) followed. Research by organisations including 5Rights Foundation documented specific **predatory design** practices in gaming, social media, and educational platforms.

> Interface design that exploits children's developmental vulnerabilities — using the same manipulation techniques as adult platforms on people who cannot yet recognise them.

**The Appeal:** From a commercial perspective, children are attractive users: they have influence over household spending, they form brand loyalties early, and they have less capacity to evaluate commercial intent. Platforms and games designed primarily for adults are also heavily used by children, and many are designed without any consideration of developmental appropriateness.

**The Friction:** [[Dark Patterns]] applied to adults exploit cognitive biases and tendencies. The same patterns applied to children exploit developmental stages that adults have moved through: children have weaker impulse control, less capacity for long-term thinking, greater susceptibility to social pressure, and less ability to distinguish advertising from content. [[Gamification Creep]] — variable rewards, streaks, progress bars — is particularly effective on children because the reward-learning system is still developing. [[Dopamine Feedback Loops]] affect children's developing brains differently than adults'. The UK Children's Code introduced 15 design standards; platforms including **TikTok**, **Instagram**, and **YouTube** were found non-compliant.

**Why This Matters:** **Predatory design** makes the child's developmental stage into a business opportunity. Once you know the specific techniques — **loot boxes**, streak mechanics, social comparison features — you can read any children's platform for what it is designed to extract.
