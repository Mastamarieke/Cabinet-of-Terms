***The unsubscribe button is grey and tiny. The stay-subscribed button is large and green. Someone made that choice.***

**Literal meaning:** **Dark patterns** are interface design choices that work against users' interests — manipulating, misleading, or coercing users into actions they would not take if the interface were honest. The term was coined by UX designer **Harry Brignull** in 2010, who began documenting specific patterns on darkpatterns.org. The site is now at deceptive.design.

**Origin:** **Brignull** introduced the concept after noticing systematic design choices across commercial websites that exploited users' cognitive tendencies and assumptions. He catalogued them by type: roach motel, sneak into basket, hidden costs, confirmshaming, trick questions, and others. The concept gained rapid traction in UX practice, academia, and — from 2021 onward — in law. In 2024, **Colin Gray**, Nataliia Bielova, Cristiana Santos, and Thomas Mildner published a comprehensive three-level ontology at ACM CHI, harmonising 10 regulatory and academic taxonomies into 65 standardised dark pattern types. The same year, Gray and colleagues documented $245 million in FTC sanctions against Epic Games for **dark patterns** in Fortnite.

> Interface design choices that work against the user — making it easy to do what the platform wants and hard to do what you want.

**The Appeal:** From a design and business perspective, **dark patterns** work. They increase conversion rates, reduce cancellations, and maximise data disclosure. The financial incentive is clear and measurable. Some patterns are so normalised — pre-ticked boxes, difficult unsubscribe flows — that they are no longer experienced as manipulation by users or designers.

**The Friction:** Gray et al.'s (2024) ontology identifies five high-level strategies: sneaking, obstruction, interface interference, forced action, and social engineering. Each has meso-level and low-level variants — 65 types in total. [[Deceptive Design]] is the academically and legally preferred term: it moves the frame from aesthetics to accountability. In 2022, **dark patterns** were codified into EU law in the Digital Services Act, the Digital Markets Act, and the California CPRA. The EDPB issued specific guidelines for **dark patterns** in social media interfaces. Courts have begun issuing sanctions. [[Predatory Design]] is the child-specific intensification. [[Nudging]] occupies the legally ambiguous zone between **dark patterns** and legitimate persuasion. [[Fair Patterns]] — developed by Marie Potel-Saville and others — is the direct design counterpart.

**Why This Matters:** **Dark patterns** name the gap between what an interface appears to offer and what it is designed to do. Once you know the vocabulary — roach motel, confirmshaming, false hierarchy — every interface becomes readable as a set of choices made by someone, for someone's benefit. Usually not yours.
