# Shadow Banning

***Your posts are still up. You can still see them. Nobody else can.***

**Literal meaning**
*Shadow* — hidden, unseen. *Banning* — restricting or blocking. **Shadow Banning** is the practice of making a user's content invisible or severely restricted without notifying the user that any action has been taken.

**Origin**
**Shadow banning** originated as a moderation technique on early internet forums in the 1990s, allowing moderators to quarantine disruptive users without provoking confrontation. It re-emerged as a major controversy when major platforms — Twitter/X, **Instagram**, **TikTok** — were accused of algorithmically suppressing content without disclosure. Savolainen (2022) documents **shadow banning** as "algorithmic folklore": a widely believed practice that platforms neither confirm nor deny.

> The silent reduction of a user's reach — content stays visible to the poster but is hidden from everyone else, without notification, explanation, or right of appeal.

**The Appeal**
Platforms need tools to limit harmful content without escalating conflict with bad actors. Invisible moderation prevents brigading and avoids rewarding rule violations with attention.

**The Friction**
Chen and Zaman (2024) demonstrate that **shadow banning** can be used to shift opinion distributions across social networks — not only to remove harmful content but to reshape discourse. The structural problem is accountability: a user cannot contest a decision they do not know has been made. [[Unblackboxing|Unblackboxing]] — making hidden mechanisms visible — is the analytical counter-practice.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see **shadow banning** as a governance mechanism rather than a content moderation tool, you see that platforms exercise significant political power over speech — invisibly and without appeal.
