# Creator Economy


***The platform is your employer. The algorithm sets your hours. The content is your labour.***

**Literal meaning:** The **creator economy** describes the commercial ecosystem in which individuals produce content for digital platforms and monetise that content through advertising revenue shares, brand partnerships, subscription products, and direct sales — with platforms functioning as the distribution and payment infrastructure.

**Origin:** The concept developed alongside **YouTube**'s Partner Programme (2007), which first enabled creators to share advertising revenue at scale. **Instagram**, **TikTok**, Twitch, Substack, and Patreon created further monetisation pathways. By 2021, research firms estimated the **creator economy** at over $100 billion in value. The term was popularised in venture capital and marketing discourse before becoming a common descriptor for a broad labour category.

> A labour model in which platforms provide the infrastructure and take the margin — where the creator owns the brand and bears all the risk.

**The Appeal:** The **creator economy** offers genuine economic opportunity and creative freedom outside traditional employment. For students in creative disciplines, the ability to build an audience and income around their work — without institutional gatekeepers — is real. Many creators have built sustainable careers. The model also democratises access to distribution in ways that legacy media structures never allowed.

**The Friction:** The employment relationship is structurally disadvantaged from the creator's side. Platforms own the distribution, set the algorithmic rules, can change monetisation terms at any time, and take 30–50% of revenue. [[Hustle Culture]] is the ideological form the labour model requires: continuous production, constant optimisation, always-on availability. [[Personal Branding]] is the identity form. The creator is simultaneously worker, manager, product, and brand — with no employment protections, no sick pay, no pension, and no collective bargaining. Research by Brooke Erin Duffy (*Not Getting Paid for It*, 2017) documented the "aspirational labour" dynamic: the majority of creators earn little or nothing, subsidising their unpaid work with hope.

**Why This Matters:** **Creator economy** names a labour model that circulates as entrepreneurship but functions as precarious employment. Once you see the employment relationship — platform as employer, creator as worker — the "freedom" framing looks different.
