# Imposter Syndrome


***Everyone else seems to know what they are doing. You feel one question away from being found out.***

**Literal meaning:** **Imposter syndrome** describes the experience of doubting your own competence and fearing exposure as a fraud — despite evidence of achievement — and attributing your success to luck, timing, or deception rather than genuine ability. The term was coined by psychologists Pauline Clance and Suzanne Imes in 1978.

**Origin:** Clance and Imes introduced the “impostor phenomenon” in a 1978 paper in _Psychotherapy: Theory, Research & Practice_, based on clinical observations of high-achieving women who felt their success was undeserved and feared being exposed as intellectual frauds. Later research extended the concept across genders, age groups, and professional contexts. Because studies use different screening tools and thresholds, there is no single reliable universal percentage; the phenomenon is better described as widely documented rather than reduced to one prevalence figure.. Digital platforms can intensify impostor feelings by turning achievement into a constant field of comparison: curated posts, success announcements, and professional visibility make others’ accomplishments appear effortless and continuous. Studies on social media comparison and online self-presentation show a connection between these dynamics and impostor feelings.

> The persistent sense of being a fraud in a space you legitimately occupy — amplified when everyone else's public presentation looks more confident than your private experience.

**The Appeal:** **Imposter syndrome** is a real and widely experienced psychological pattern. Naming it is useful: knowing that the experience is common, has a name, and has been studied can reduce its intensity. The clinical literature offers effective interventions. The term provides validation and community for people who feel isolated in their self-doubt.

**The Friction:** The digital context amplifies the original phenomenon. [[Comparison Culture]] — permanent ambient comparison as a platform feature — provides a continuous stream of others' curated achievements. [[Highlight Reel]] — showing only the best — means that what you compare your internal experience to is others' external presentations: their best work, their most confident moments, their most impressive outcomes. The comparison is structurally unfair, and the unfairness is designed in. [[Personal Branding]] — treating yourself as a product — intensifies the dynamic: students are explicitly instructed to present their most polished self, which simultaneously worsens everyone else's **imposter syndrome**.

**Why This Matters:** **Imposter syndrome** makes visible the gap between internal experience and external presentation — and the specific harm that social media does to that gap. Once you know that everyone's backstage is messier than their **highlight reel**, the comparison basis changes.
