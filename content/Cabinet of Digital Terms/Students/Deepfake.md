# Deepfake


***The video shows her saying it. She never said it. You cannot tell from watching.***

**Literal meaning:** A **deepfake** is a synthetic media product — typically video or audio — in which a real person's likeness, voice, or both are used to generate content they did not produce. The term combines "deep learning" (the AI technique) with "fake." The most common applications include political disinformation (fabricated speeches), non-consensual intimate imagery (NCII), and fraud (voice cloning for financial scams).

**Origin:** The term originated in 2017 on Reddit, where a user called "deepfakes" posted synthetic pornographic videos using celebrities' faces. The technique — face-swapping via generative adversarial networks (GANs) — was adapted from academic computer vision research. By 2019, **deepfake** creation tools had become consumer-accessible. By 2023–2024, voice cloning and full video synthesis had become commercially available, dramatically lowering the technical barrier. Research by Hany Farid at Berkeley has been central to both detection methodology and policy advocacy.

> AI-generated media in which a real person appears to say or do something they never did — accessible to anyone with a laptop, and increasingly indistinguishable from real.

**The Appeal:** **Deepfake** technology has legitimate creative, educational, and commercial applications: film restoration, language dubbing, historical reconstruction, and **accessibility** features (voice synthesis). The underlying capability — realistic human face and voice generation — is technically remarkable and broadly useful.

**The Friction:** The harms are asymmetric and gendered. Research consistently documents that the overwhelming majority of **deepfake** content is non-consensual intimate imagery — synthetic pornography created using the faces of real women without consent. [[Synthetic Generation]] is the broader category; **deepfake** is the specific harmful application to real named individuals. [[Dehumanization]] — the denial of full humanity — is what non-consensual intimate imagery systematically performs. For students, deepfakes present specific challenges: fabricated evidence in disputes, sextortion using synthetic images, and the erosion of epistemic trust that makes all video evidence uncertain.

**Why This Matters:** **Deepfake** names the specific harm of having your likeness used without consent to create content you never produced. Once the technology is widely accessible, "seeing is believing" is no longer a reliable epistemological standard. The uncertainty that creates is itself a harm, independent of any specific fabrication.
