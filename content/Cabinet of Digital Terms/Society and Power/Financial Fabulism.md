# Financial Fabulism


***The valuation does not reflect the revenue. The revenue does not reflect the users. People lost real money.***

> Financial claims that systematically exceed reality — not technically fraud, but a genre in which the gap between claim and fact is the product.

**Literal meaning:** **Financial fabulism** describes the practice — common in tech and startup culture — of making financial and capability claims that significantly exceed verifiable reality, without crossing the legal threshold of fraud. The fabulist builds a narrative of future value that sustains investment, press coverage, and public attention regardless of current performance.

**Origin:** The term was used by journalist Tim O'Reilly and has circulated in tech journalism and criticism to describe a pattern more systematic than individual exaggeration: an entire genre of corporate communication in which projected capabilities, user numbers, and valuations routinely exceed what evidence supports — and in which the genre conventions are understood by sophisticated participants while misleading ordinary investors and users.

>The term **Financial Fabulism** emerged in critiques of Silicon Valley startup culture and speculative venture capitalism, describing systems in which storytelling, founder mythology, and valuation narratives become economically productive independent of material performance.

**The Appeal:** **Financial fabulism** produces real capital. If investors believe the narrative, they fund the company, which may eventually produce the capabilities the narrative promised — making the fabulism retrospectively accurate. Silicon Valley's success stories are largely stories of this kind: Amazon, Google, and Tesla all sustained narratives that exceeded present reality for extended periods before the reality caught up.

**The Friction:** The genre has victims. [[Memecoin]] is the retail investor version: speculation on narratives that are explicitly acknowledged as having no intrinsic value. [[Grifters]] are the criminal terminus: the genre conventions of **financial fabulism** provide cover for outright fraud — the same communication style, the same future-value narratives, with no intention of delivery.  is the analytical instrument: asking where on the nine-point maturity scale a claimed capability actually sits immediately distinguishes fabulism from substance. [[Techno King]] is one persona through which fabulism is performed: the ironic register signals that normal valuation conventions do not apply.

**Why This Matters:** **Financial fabulism** names the genre that makes ordinary accountability metrics inapplicable to certain companies and individuals. Once you see it as a genre with conventions, you can ask: what would this claim look like in a standard financial disclosure? The gap between those two descriptions is the measure of the fabulism.
