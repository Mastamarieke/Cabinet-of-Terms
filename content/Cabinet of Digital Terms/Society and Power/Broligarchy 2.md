# Broligarchy


***The consolidation of power at the top of the tech industry has a social structure. It looks like a friend group.***

**Literal meaning:** **Broligarchy** combines "bro" (informal male friendship, specifically the homosocial culture of tech industry masculinity) with "oligarchy" (rule by a small wealthy elite) — describing the social formation in which a small number of tech billionaires exercise political power through a network of personal relationships, shared cultural references, and mutual reinforcement.

**Origin:** The term circulated in political commentary from 2024–2025, gaining analytical traction as the political alignment of multiple tech billionaires — Musk, Thiel, Andreessen, Zuckerberg, Bezos — became explicit around the US 2024 election. The concept names the social infrastructure of tech political power: not merely that wealthy tech founders have political influence, but that this influence operates through a specific male bonding culture that reinforces shared values and coordinates action without formal organisation.

> A social formation in which tech oligarchic power operates through male bonding networks — where friendship and power are structurally inseparable.

**The Appeal:** Social networks among powerful people have always shaped political outcomes. The **broligarchy** is not a conspiracy — it is a social fact, legible in public statements, investment patterns, and political donations. Naming it is not paranoia; it is sociology.

**The Friction:** [[Muskism]] is the individual expression; **broligarchy** is the social network that makes it a class formation rather than an individual phenomenon. [[Revolving Door]] personnel circulate within the **broligarchy**'s orbit: the boundaries between tech companies, investment firms, and political actors are maintained by the same social network. [[Founder Mode]] and [[Objectivism (Rand)]] are the shared ideological resources. [[Cyberfeminism]] — and its predecessors — has documented the gendered structure of technology power for decades: the **broligarchy** is the current form of what **Haraway**'s **Cyborg** Manifesto was already contesting in 1985.

**Why This Matters:** **Broligarchy** names the social dimension of tech political power — which is important because social networks are harder to regulate than formal organisations. Once you see it as a network rather than a conspiracy, the question shifts from "who is coordinating?" to "what values does the network share, and how does the network reproduce itself?"
