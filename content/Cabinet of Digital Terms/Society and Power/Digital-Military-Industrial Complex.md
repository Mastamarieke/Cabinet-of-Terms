***The same companies running your email are running the military's logistics.***

**Literal meaning:** The **digital-military-industrial complex** describes the structural entanglement of large technology companies, military and intelligence agencies, and the political system — extending Eisenhower's original concept into the domain of cloud computing, AI, surveillance infrastructure, and data systems.

**Origin:** The extension of Eisenhower's concept to digital technology was accelerated by revelations from Edward Snowden's 2013 disclosures, which documented the depth of collaboration between major US technology companies and the NSA. Subsequent developments — Amazon's JEDI cloud contract with the Pentagon, Microsoft's IVAS augmented reality contract for US Army, Google's Project Maven (and subsequent withdrawal following employee protest), **Palantir**'s pervasive government contracts — made the structural relationship visible and named.

> The structural entanglement of Big Tech infrastructure with military and intelligence operations — where the same systems run civilian life and warfare.

**The Appeal:** Military and government use of advanced technology is legitimate and necessary. Cloud infrastructure, AI systems, and data analytics are as relevant to defence as to commerce. Technology companies have capabilities that defence establishments need. The collaboration produces genuine operational value.

**The Friction:** [[Military-Industrial Complex]] created the structural pressure toward military expenditure regardless of strategic necessity; the digital version creates the same pressure in AI, surveillance, and data infrastructure. [[Revolving Door]] is the personnel mechanism: executives move between tech companies, the Pentagon, and intelligence agencies. [[Dual-Use Technology]] is the product mechanism: civilian AI systems are also military AI systems by capability. [[Sovereignty as a Service]] is the governance consequence: states that depend on commercially provided digital infrastructure have effectively contracted out aspects of their defence capability to private entities. The "Adding amplitude to war is obviously an issue" quote — McClendon on **Niantic**'s LGM — is the dual-use problem stated without resolution.

**Why This Matters:** **Digital-military-industrial complex** names the structural relationship that makes "tech for good" and "tech for war" the same infrastructure. Once you see the entanglement, the question of whether a technology company is a civilian or military actor becomes structurally ambiguous rather than a matter of corporate choice.
