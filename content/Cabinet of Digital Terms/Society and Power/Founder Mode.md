# Founder Mode


***The founder knows best. The governance structures are friction. The employees should get out of the way.***

**Literal meaning:** **Founder mode** is a concept introduced by Paul Graham in a September 2024 essay, arguing that company founders should manage directly across all levels of the organisation — bypassing conventional management hierarchies — rather than delegating to professional managers who do not share the founder's vision and authority. Graham argued this is why founder-led companies outperform professionally managed ones.

**Origin:** Graham published "**Founder Mode**" on paulgraham.com in September 2024, following a talk by Brian Chesky of Airbnb. The essay was immediately widely discussed in Silicon Valley and beyond. It provided ideological vocabulary for a governance claim that many tech founders had already been making in practice: that founder authority should not be constrained by conventional corporate governance, board oversight, or professional management structures.

> A Silicon Valley leadership ideology in which founders reject governance structures as friction — and in which personal authority substitutes for institutional accountability.

**The Appeal:** **Founder mode** names something real: founders who are deeply engaged in product and operations often do make better decisions than professional managers who lack their institutional knowledge and commitment. The track record of some founder-managed companies supports the argument empirically.

**The Friction:** **Founder mode** as ideology extends beyond the company to the state. [[Muskism]] is **founder mode** applied to government: the founder's direct authority, contempt for administrative process, and bypass of institutional governance — applied to public agencies rather than startups. [[Hubris Syndrome]] is what **founder mode** produces when the accountability structures that would constrain it are successfully removed. [[Objectivism (Rand)]] provides the philosophical substrate: the exceptional individual's judgment is inherently superior to institutional constraint. The argument's specific rhetorical function is to provide a success narrative for the removal of corporate governance — making accountability structures look like management incompetence.

**Why This Matters:** **Founder mode** names an ideology that makes the removal of governance look like operational excellence. Once you see the move — accountability structures framed as bureaucratic obstacles — you can identify it wherever it appears: in companies, in government agencies, and in arguments for why exceptional leaders should not be constrained by ordinary rules.
