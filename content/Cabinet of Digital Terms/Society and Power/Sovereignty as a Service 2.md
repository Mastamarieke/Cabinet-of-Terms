# Sovereignty as a Service


***The state runs on AWS. The military uses Microsoft. Sovereignty has been outsourced.***

**Literal meaning:** **Sovereignty as a Service** describes the condition in which a state's exercise of sovereign functions — communications, tax collection, benefits administration, border control, defence logistics — is dependent on private technology infrastructure that the state does not own and cannot fully control.

**Origin:** The term adapts the cloud computing "as a service" model (Software as a Service, Infrastructure as a Service) to describe a political condition: sovereignty that is rented rather than owned. It describes a pattern documented across multiple governments that have outsourced critical administrative and intelligence functions to private vendors — most prominently **Palantir**, Microsoft, and Amazon — without retaining the capacity to perform those functions independently.

> A condition in which state sovereignty is operationally dependent on private infrastructure — where the government cannot function without a vendor it cannot fire.

**The Appeal:** Outsourcing state functions to private technology providers offers real capabilities at lower cost than in-house development. The expertise, scale, and investment capacity of major technology companies genuinely exceeds what most governments can develop internally. The efficiency argument is real.

**The Friction:** [[Vendor Lock-in]] is the structural mechanism: once state functions are built on private infrastructure, exit costs are prohibitive. [[CLOUD Act]] is the legal dimension: US cloud providers must comply with US government data requests, meaning that states using US cloud infrastructure have in effect made their data subject to US jurisdiction. [[Technofeudalism]] is the broader category: **Sovereignty as a Service** is what feudal dependency looks like when the lords are technology companies and the serfs are governments. [[Muskism]] is the most acute form: a private individual with control over communications infrastructure, satellite internet, and space launch capacity who simultaneously advises government departments.

**Why This Matters:** **Sovereignty as a Service** names the political condition produced when efficiency arguments consistently win procurement decisions. Once you see the dependency accumulating, "the vendor has access to the data" is not a data security problem — it is a sovereignty problem.
