# Deadnaming


***The old name is still indexed. The search engine does not forget.***

**Literal meaning:** **Deadnaming** refers to the use of a transgender person's birth name — the name they used before transition, which they have abandoned — either deliberately as a form of harassment, or inadvertently through digital systems that retain and surface old data. The "dead" name is the one the person no longer uses or identifies with.

**Origin:** The term [[Deadnaming]] originated in trans communities as a vital act of respect, entering mainstream discourse around 2015. But what began as dignity has hardened into **digital violence**: search engines, cached pages, and news archives retain old names indefinitely, so a trans person's past is never truly past. While the **Right to be Forgotten** (GDPR Article 17) promises relief, Ambrose and Ausloos (2013) warn its implementation pits privacy against public interest — and privacy usually loses. For trans people, this is existential: Haimson et al. (2019) show that failed data protection forces them into perpetual exposure, their true selves erased while deadnames remain permanently indexed. The same logic of digital erasure silences others: Gaza-based journalist **Bisan Owda** had her **TikTok** permanently banned and her **Instagram** shadow-banned after a change in platform ownership — her current voice deleted, her past footage archived and weaponized. Whether it's a deadname that won't disappear or a living voice that does, the pattern is the same: platforms and systems decide whose identity survives and whose is extinguished.

> The persistence of a person's abandoned name in digital systems — where technical infrastructure denies the right to be forgotten.

**The Appeal:** From a data architecture perspective, name changes are a known problem with multiple contexts — marriage, legal name change, witness protection — and existing systems handle them imperfectly for everyone. The technical difficulty is real: cached pages, third-party databases, and cross-platform data sharing make comprehensive name removal genuinely hard.

**The Friction:** The technical difficulty is not the only issue. Deliberate **deadnaming** — using a trans person's birth name to deny their identity, typically in political discourse or harassment campaigns — is a distinct practice from inadvertent retention. [[Algorithmic Violence]] — harm caused by automated systems without individual human decisions — describes what happens when search algorithms surface old names: no person chose to deadname the individual, but the harm is identical. [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — human behaviour as raw material for commercial products — created the systems that retain and surface this data. [[Dehumanization]] research documents that denial of identity is a precursor to more serious harms. The GDPR right to be forgotten exists in European law; its enforcement against global platforms remains inconsistent.

**Why This Matters:** **Deadnaming** makes visible the specific vulnerability of identity at the intersection of digital permanence and trans existence — and, more broadly, the way that data systems designed for commercial purposes produce identity harms as a structural side effect.
