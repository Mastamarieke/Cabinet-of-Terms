***Medical vocabulary, applied to an identity. The scientific framing does the work the evidence cannot.***

**Literal meaning:** **Social contagion**, in its clinical use, describes how certain behaviours or conditions can spread through social networks — self-harm, eating disorders, suicide. In contemporary political discourse, the term has been applied specifically to trans identity: the claim that young people are identifying as transgender not from genuine gender dysphoria but because social influence, online communities, and peer pressure have "spread" the identity like a contagion.

**Origin:** The term “**social contagion**” originally comes from sociology and psychology, where it describes how behaviors, emotions, beliefs, or social trends can spread through groups and networks in ways similar to contagion. In recent years, the concept became heavily politicized in online debates surrounding gender identity, particularly through discussions of “Rapid-Onset Gender Dysphoria” (ROGD). In 2018, researcher Lisa Littman published a study based on parental reports suggesting that peer groups and online communities might influence adolescents’ experiences of gender dysphoria. The study became widely cited in political and online discourse as evidence for the idea of gender identity as a form of **social contagion**. However, the methodology and framing of the study were strongly criticized by researchers such as Arjee Restar, who argued that the research relied on selective sampling and risked reinforcing stigmatizing narratives about transgender people.

> The application of epidemiological framing to trans identity — borrowing medical authority to make a political claim.

**The Appeal:** The framing addresses a real demographic change: the number of young people identifying as trans or non-binary has increased significantly. Concerns about young people's wellbeing are genuine. The contagion model provides a causal explanation that many parents and commentators find intuitively plausible, and the clinical vocabulary lends it authority.

**The Friction:** The scientific basis is contested at best. The ROGD concept has been rejected by major medical organisations including the American Academy of Pediatrics and the World Professional Association for Transgender Health. [[Dehumanization]] research — specifically Bandura's moral disengagement framework — shows that pathologising an identity group systematically legitimises action against it. [[Woke Mind Virus]] applies the same contagion logic to political positions; **social contagion** applies it to identity. Both borrow clinical authority to reframe a political target as a medical problem requiring intervention rather than an argument requiring engagement. [[Groomer]] completes the circuit: if identity spreads like contagion, those who promote it become vectors.

**Why This Matters:** **Social contagion** makes visible how scientific-sounding language can function as political rhetoric. The question to ask of any contagion claim is: what evidence standard was applied, and who benefits from the framing?
