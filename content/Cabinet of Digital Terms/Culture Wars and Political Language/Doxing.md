**Literal meaning**
*Dox* — from *docs* (documents): to research and publish private information about a person. **Doxing** is the deliberate exposure of private personal information — address, workplace, phone number, family members — to enable harassment or intimidation.

**Origin**
**Doxing** emerged from hacker culture in the 1990s as a technique for exposing anonymous adversaries. Douglas (2016) established its conceptual taxonomy: de-anonymising **doxing** (revealing identity), targeting **doxing** (enabling physical location), and delegitimising **doxing** (damaging reputation). It became systematised as a tool of coordinated harassment during GamerGate (2014) and has since been weaponised in political campaigns, culture war conflicts, and state-adjacent operations.

> The targeted publication of private personal information to enable harassment, intimidation, or harm — requiring no technical expertise, only motivation.

**The Appeal**
In its original hacker context, **doxing** exposed genuinely harmful anonymous actors. Accountability for powerful people who abuse anonymity is a legitimate goal.

**The Friction**
Douglas (2016) and Chen et al. (2019) document that **doxing** is overwhelmingly used against private individuals and marginalised groups rather than powerful actors. The information required is often publicly available — social media profiles, company websites, electoral registers — assembled into a weapon. [[Dehumanization|Dehumanization]] is frequently the ideological precondition: the target must first be framed as deserving exposure.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see **doxing** as infrastructure rather than an individual act, you see that the aggregation of public data into private weapons is a structural feature of the open web — and that protection requires systemic design responses, not just individual privacy hygiene.
