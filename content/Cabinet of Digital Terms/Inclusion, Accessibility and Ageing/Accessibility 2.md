# Accessibility


***The website has an accessibility statement. The form cannot be completed with a screen reader.***

**Literal meaning:** **Accessibility**, in digital contexts, describes the degree to which digital products, services, and interfaces can be used by people with disabilities — visual, auditory, motor, cognitive. It is codified in technical standards (WCAG — Web Content **Accessibility** Guidelines) and law (the European **Accessibility** Act 2019, the Americans with Disabilities Act).

**Origin:** The concept developed alongside the disability rights movement from the 1970s. The World Wide Web Consortium's Web **Accessibility** Initiative launched in 1997, producing the first WCAG guidelines in 1999. The current standard, WCAG 2.1, defines four principles: perceivable, operable, understandable, and robust. The EU Web **Accessibility** Directive (2016) and the European **Accessibility** Act (2019) made compliance legally mandatory for public sector and, progressively, commercial digital services.

> A technical and legal standard for digital inclusion — that names a floor below which design becomes discriminatory.

**The Appeal:** **Accessibility** standards produce measurable, auditable improvements. Screen reader compatibility, keyboard navigation, colour contrast ratios, and alt text are specific, testable requirements. For disabled users who depend on these features, the difference between compliant and non-compliant design is the difference between access and exclusion.

**The Friction:** Compliance with the standard is not the same as genuine inclusion. WCAG defines a minimum — what the term claims is access; what it delivers is often grudging adequacy. [[Universal Design]] — design for everyone from the start — is the aspiration that **accessibility** requirements often fail to reach. [[Inclusive Design]] is the methodology that addresses the gap: designing from the margin, not retrofitting from the centre. [[Digital Exclusion]] remains substantial even in technically compliant systems: a website can pass an **accessibility** audit and still be unusable for someone with cognitive disabilities, low digital literacy, or limited connectivity.

**Why This Matters:** **Accessibility** makes visible the gap between compliance and genuine inclusion. The standard is a floor, not a ceiling. Once you know the difference, "this site is accessible" is the beginning of the question, not the answer.
