# Inclusive Design


***Designing for the person with the greatest need produces a solution that works better for everyone else.***

**Literal meaning:** **Inclusive design** is a design methodology that begins by identifying and centring the needs of people who are typically excluded — people with disabilities, older users, non-native speakers, people with low digital literacy — on the basis that solutions designed for the margins produce better products for everyone.

**Origin:** The methodology was significantly developed by Kat Holmes, whose book *Mismatch: How Exclusion Shapes Design* (2018, MIT Press) became a foundational text. Holmes, formerly at Microsoft, argued that exclusion is always a design failure — and that designing for excluded users reveals design problems that affect all users. Microsoft's **inclusive design** toolkit, developed with Holmes, explicitly uses disability and edge cases as design drivers. The approach builds on **Universal Design** but shifts the methodology: rather than aiming for a universal standard, it starts from specific excluded perspectives.

> A design methodology that starts from excluded users — on the basis that solutions produced at the margins work better for everyone.

**The Appeal:** **Inclusive design** has a strong evidence base. Voice control, developed for users with motor disabilities, transformed how everyone interacts with devices. Email and SMS, designed in part for users who struggle with voice communication, reshaped how everyone communicates. The curb cut effect — design solutions for disabled users improving everyone's experience — is well-documented and repeatable.

**The Friction:** **Inclusive design** requires that excluded users are not just consulted but are active participants in the design process. [[Accessibility]] adds features for disabled users after core design decisions are made; **inclusive design** requires that disabled users are present when those decisions are being made. [[Universal Design]] is the philosophical aspiration; **inclusive design** is the operational methodology. The structural barrier remains: most design organisations do not employ the people they most need to include, and **inclusive design** cannot be practised from a distance.

**Why This Matters:** **Inclusive design** names a principle that is counter-intuitive to most design practice: start with the hardest case, not the average case. Once you know that principle, you can ask of any design: who was the hardest user we designed for?
