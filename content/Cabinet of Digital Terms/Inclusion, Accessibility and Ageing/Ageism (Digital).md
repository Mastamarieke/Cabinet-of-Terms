***The interface was not designed to exclude older users. It was designed without them.***

**Literal meaning:** Digital ageism describes age-based discrimination as it manifests in digital systems — through interface design that assumes peak-capacity young adult users, policy decisions that exclude older people from digital service design, and language that frames older users as exceptions, problems, or afterthoughts.

**Origin:** The concept extends Robert Butler's foundational definition of ageism (1969) — systematic stereotyping and discrimination against people because they are old — into digital contexts. Researcher Eugène Loos and colleagues developed the specific concept of digital ageism through empirical studies of older users' experiences with digital systems, published from around 2013. The WHO's 2021 global report on ageism provided a broader institutional framework.

> Age-based discrimination embedded in digital design, policy, and language — producing systematic exclusion that is rarely named as discrimination.

**The Appeal:** Framing exclusion as ageism rather than as individual inability to adapt shifts the locus of responsibility. It is not that older users cannot learn; it is that systems were not designed with them in mind, and calling this discrimination rather than a skills gap opens different policy and design responses.

**The Friction:** Digital ageism is embedded at multiple levels. At the design level: interfaces that require fast reaction times, small touch targets, complex navigation, and frequent updates. At the organisational level: design teams that skew young and do not include or consult older users. At the language level: [[Silver Surfer]] framing that celebrates older users as exceptions. At the policy level: service redesign that replaces accessible physical channels with digital-only alternatives without transition support. [[Tech Abandonment]] is the direct behavioural consequence. [[Digital Exclusion]] is the systemic outcome. [[Digital Ageing]] is the structural context: the design problem compounds as the population ages.

**Why This Matters:** Digital ageism makes visible that exclusion is a design choice, not a demographic inevitability. Once you name it as discrimination, the question is not "why can't older people keep up?" but "who is being protected by keeping older people out of the room when design decisions are made?"
