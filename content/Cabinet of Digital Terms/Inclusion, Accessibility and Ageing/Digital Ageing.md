***You did not choose to grow older in a world that redesigns its interfaces every two years.***

**Literal meaning:** **Digital ageing** describes the experience of growing older within a society that is increasingly organised around digital systems — navigating healthcare, banking, government services, social connection, and daily life through interfaces that were not designed with ageing users in mind, and that change faster than adaptation can keep pace with.

**Origin:** The term developed in gerontology and human-computer interaction research from the early 2010s, as digital service delivery replaced physical service points at scale. Key researchers include Eugène Loos (Universiteit van Amsterdam), who documented the specific challenges of **digital ageing** through empirical studies of older users in the Netherlands, and Joseph Coughlin (MIT AgeLab), whose work on technology and ageing influenced both academic and policy discourse.

> The experience of navigating an increasingly digital society as the body and cognitive capacity change — not a choice but a condition.

**The Appeal:** Framing ageing and digital life together — rather than treating older people as a special case — produces better design. When designers consider the full lifespan, they are forced to confront assumptions about hand-eye coordination, reaction time, cognitive load, and visual acuity that current interfaces routinely violate for users of all ages.

**The Friction:** **Digital ageing** is not a temporary problem that will resolve as "digital natives" get older. Current sixty-year-olds grew up with computers; current eighty-year-olds will be today's fifty-year-olds. The interfaces of 2040 will be as unfamiliar to today's fifty-year-olds as current interfaces are to today's eighty-year-olds. [[Ageism (Digital)]] is the structural backdrop: if older users are not valued as users, their needs will not drive design decisions. [[Tech Abandonment]] is the behavioural consequence: systems that demand too much produce withdrawal. [[Silver Surfer]] is the patronising positive framing that makes the problem invisible by celebrating the exception.

**Why This Matters:** **Digital ageing** makes visible that the relationship between people and technology changes over a lifetime — and that systems designed for peak-capacity adults exclude people at both ends of that curve. The design question is not "how do we accommodate older users?" but "why did we design for peak capacity in the first place?"
