# Digital Divide


***The government service moved online. Not everyone can get online.***

**Literal meaning:** The **digital divide** describes the gap between those who have adequate access to digital technologies — devices, connectivity, skills, and meaningful use — and those who do not. The term encompasses multiple layers: access (can you get online?), skills (can you use what you access?), and outcomes (does access translate into benefit?).

**Origin:** The term entered policy discourse in the mid-1990s, when US Commerce Department reports documented unequal computer and internet access along racial and income lines. The NTIA's 1995 report *Falling Through the Net* established the framework. Subsequent research — particularly by Jan van Dijk, whose *The Deepening Divide* (2005) produced the most comprehensive theoretical framework — documented that the divide was not simply about physical access but about multiple reinforcing inequalities: motivational, physical, skills-based, and usage-based.

> Multi-layered inequality in access to digital infrastructure — where physical connectivity is only the first gap, and skills, affordability, and relevance are the rest.

**The Appeal:** The **digital divide** framing has driven substantial policy investment: broadband infrastructure programmes, device access initiatives, and digital literacy programmes. The frame successfully mobilised political will around a recognisable inequality.

**The Friction:** The simple access frame — do you have a connection? — has been largely addressed in high-income countries, while the deeper divides have widened. Van Dijk's research documented that as physical access became more universal, skills gaps and usage gaps became the primary determinants of digital inequality. [[Digital Exclusion]] names the active form: not being left behind but being pushed out by design choices. [[Ageism (Digital)]] is one structural driver. [[Digital Colonialism]] — data centres in the Global South consuming local resources without local benefit — is the international dimension. [[Privacy as a Premium]] — surveillance as the price of poverty — is the economic dimension within high-income societies.

**Why This Matters:** **Digital divide** makes visible that access is necessary but not sufficient. Once you see the multiple layers — access, skills, meaningful use, benefit — the political aspiration of "getting everyone online" looks like the beginning of a much larger project.
