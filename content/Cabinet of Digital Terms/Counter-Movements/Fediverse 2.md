# Fediverse

***You left Twitter because of Musk. You joined Mastodon. Then Meta arrived there too.***

**Literal meaning:** The **fediverse** (federated universe) is a network of independently operated social media servers that communicate with each other through shared open protocols — primarily **ActivityPub**. Unlike centralised platforms, no single company owns or controls the **fediverse**. **Mastodon** (microblogging), **PeerTube** (video), **Pixelfed** (images) and dozens of other platforms can all communicate across server boundaries, the way email works across providers: a Gmail user can email a Hotmail user without either knowing or caring about the infrastructure behind the other.

**Origin:** The **fediverse** emerged from open-source and decentralisation movements of the early 2000s, building on protocols like OStatus before **ActivityPub** became the dominant standard (W3C, 2018). It remained a niche technical community until 2022–2023, when **Elon Musk**'s acquisition of Twitter triggered a mass exodus that brought hundreds of thousands of new users to **Mastodon** and related platforms. The University of Groningen, public broadcasters, and institutions including PublicSpaces.net began operating their own **fediverse** servers as part of a broader move toward digital sovereignty.

> A decentralised network of independently operated social media servers, connected through open protocols — built as an alternative to platform monopolies, and now being entered by those same monopolies.

**The Appeal:** The **fediverse** returns structural control to users and institutions. You choose your server — and therefore your rules, your moderation policy, your data governance. If you dislike your server, you can migrate your account and take your followers with you. No algorithm decides what you see. No advertiser pays to reach you. No single company can change the terms of service overnight. For public institutions, universities, and civil society organisations, running your own **fediverse** server is an act of [[Sovereignty as a Service]] reversed: taking back what was outsourced to platform monopolies.

**The Friction:** Three structural vulnerabilities undermine the promise. First, governance: with no central authority, content moderation depends on thousands of individual server administrators. Communities most likely to be harassed end up doing the most moderation work (**Sinnreich** et al., 2025). Second, commercial capture: Meta's **Threads** platform already uses **ActivityPub**, meaning it can communicate with **Mastodon** — and now has a vested interest in shaping the protocol's future. [[Surveillance Capitalism]] does not disappear from a federated network; it enters through the server with the most users. Third, the technical barrier: choosing a server, understanding federation, managing an account across instances — these are skills that reproduce existing digital inequalities. The **fediverse** is open in principle and inaccessible in practice for many. As **Sinnreich** puts it: "Democratized tech doesn't guarantee democratic outcomes."

**Why This Matters:** The **fediverse** makes the architecture of platforms visible by contrast. Every design choice that feels natural on **Instagram** — algorithmic feeds, advertising, data extraction, no exit — is revealed as a choice, not a necessity, the moment you encounter a system built differently. Whether the **fediverse** survives commercial capture or quietly becomes another layer of the same infrastructure is the open question. The answer depends not on the protocol but on who governs it.
