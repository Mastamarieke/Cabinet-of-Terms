***Physical presence now needs a name to distinguish itself from the default.***

**Literal meaning**
*IRL* — abbreviation for *In Real Life*. Originally used in online communities from the early 1990s to distinguish offline from online experience. The term assumes that digital life is the unmarked baseline and physical life requires specification.

**Origin**
IRL entered internet vocabulary on Usenet groups and IRC channels in the early 1990s as online communities grew large enough that members needed to distinguish offline interactions from their online relationships. Turkle (2011) analysed the psychological consequences of this framing in *Alone Together*: when online becomes the default, physical presence becomes the marked, special case. Stedman (2020) documents IRL as a search for authenticity and belonging in response to digital saturation.

> The term that reveals digital dominance by requiring physical reality to announce itself — used since Usenet in the 1990s, now more loaded than it was then.

**The Appeal**
IRL names a genuine need: physical presence, embodied connection, and environments free from digital demands. The counter-movements it generates address real deprivation.

**The Friction**
The term's existence is its own argument: that physical reality needs a label to distinguish it from default digital life marks a significant cultural shift. [[Digital Detox]] and [[Unplugging|Unplugging]] share the same logic — and the same recuperation risk: IRL counter-movements are frequently organised and distributed through the platforms they critique.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see IRL as a symptom of digital dominance rather than just a term, you see that the proliferation of IRL counter-movements — phone-free schools, offline therapy, social prescribing — is the cultural system attempting to correct an imbalance it cannot name structurally.
