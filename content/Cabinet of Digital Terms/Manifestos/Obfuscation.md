**Literal meaning:** **Obfuscation** is the deliberate practice of making information, systems, or processes difficult to understand or trace. In digital contexts, it describes both a corporate strategy — making surveillance infrastructure, algorithmic decision-making, and data practices opaque to users and regulators — and a potential counter-practice — generating noise to confuse surveillance systems.

**Origin:** The concept was developed analytically by Finn Brunton and Helen Nissenbaum in *Obfuscation: A User's Guide for Privacy and Protest* (2015, MIT Press). They traced the history of **obfuscation** as both a power strategy and a resistance tactic — from radar chaff deployed to confuse enemy systems to AdNauseam browser extensions that click all advertisements to confuse tracking profiles.

> The deliberate production of opacity — used by powerful actors to evade accountability, and potentially by individuals to protect privacy.

**The Appeal:** **Obfuscation** as resistance is a real strategy. When surveillance is unavoidable, generating noise — false data, contradictory signals, irrelevant clicks — can degrade the surveillance system's accuracy. Brunton and Nissenbaum document its use by activists, minorities under surveillance, and privacy-conscious users. The asymmetry of the practice is important: the powerful obfuscate to protect systems; the less powerful obfuscate to protect themselves.

**The Friction:** Corporate **obfuscation** vastly outscales user **obfuscation**. [[Dark Patterns]] — interface design that misleads — are a form of **obfuscation** deployed at scale against users. [[Privacy Washing]] — privacy rhetoric without corresponding practice — is **obfuscation** in language. [[Black Box]] is **obfuscation** as architecture: the system is designed so that its internal processes cannot be inspected. [[Surveillance Capitalism]] — human behaviour as raw material — depends on obfuscating the data collection from the people being collected. The asymmetry runs through all of these: **obfuscation** is most available to those with the most resources to deploy it.

**Why This Matters:** **Obfuscation** makes visible that opacity is a choice with a direction — it protects someone from someone else. Once you ask who is being protected from whom by any given opacity, the politics of the system becomes legible.
