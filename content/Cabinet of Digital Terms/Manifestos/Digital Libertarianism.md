# Digital Libertarianism


***Barlow declared cyberspace free in 1996. The declaration was made before the platforms arrived.***

**Literal meaning:** **Digital libertarianism** is the ideological position that cyberspace should be — and to some extent inherently is — beyond the jurisdiction of governments and states, self-governing through the norms of its users, and structured by freedom from regulation rather than by law.

**Origin:** **John Perry Barlow**'s 1996 *Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace* — written in Davos and addressed directly to "Governments of the Industrial World" — is the founding text. It declared: cyberspace does not lie within your borders, you have no sovereignty where we gather, we will create our own Social Contract. **Barlow** was a Grateful Dead lyricist and co-founder of the Electronic Frontier Foundation. The declaration was widely shared and became the ideological template for early internet culture.

> The founding ideology of the early internet — declaring cyberspace beyond state jurisdiction and self-regulating — that became the upstream assumption of platform capitalism.

**The Appeal:** **Barlow**'s declaration addressed genuine concerns: government censorship, surveillance, and the potential destruction of online communities by heavy-handed regulation. The impulse to protect the internet from state control was not paranoid — it was prescient in several respects.

**The Friction:** The vacuum that **digital libertarianism** produced was not filled by citizen freedom. It was filled by corporate platforms — which are not states, and therefore technically outside **Barlow**'s critique, but which exercise forms of control and extraction he never anticipated. [[Muskism]] — Slobodian & Tarnoff's 2026 characterisation of Musk's governance vision — is **digital libertarianism**'s contemporary inheritor, updated for the platform era. [[Objectivism (Rand)]] is the philosophical upstream: the same framework of individual sovereignty against collective authority, applied to digital space. [[Technofeudalism]] is the outcome: not the free cyberspace **Barlow** envisioned but a landscape of corporate fiefdoms each with their own rules and no democratic accountability.

**Why This Matters:** **Digital libertarianism** names the founding ideology of the internet's governance vacuum. Once you know the text, you can read a great deal of contemporary platform politics as variations on the same theme: the state is the problem; the private sector is not.
