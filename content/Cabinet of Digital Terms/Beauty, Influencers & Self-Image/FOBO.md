# FOBO


***You chose the restaurant. Then you checked the one next door. You are still not sure.***

**Literal meaning:** **FOBO** — Fear of Better Options — describes the anxiety and indecision produced by the awareness that there may always be a better alternative available: a better restaurant, a better partner, a better product, a better experience. The term is the counterpart to **FOMO** (Fear of Missing Out): where **FOMO** is anxiety about absence, **FOBO** is anxiety about premature commitment.

**Origin:** The term was coined by Patrick McGinnis — who also coined **FOMO** — in a 2004 Harvard Business School publication. It entered wider cultural vocabulary in the 2010s as recommendation platforms, dating apps, and e-commerce made the pool of apparent alternatives effectively infinite. McGinnis expanded on both concepts in *Fear of Missing Out* (2020).

> The paralysis of infinite choice — where the possibility of a better option makes any commitment feel premature.

**The Appeal:** **FOBO** responds to something real. When options genuinely multiply — when there are thousands of restaurants, millions of potential partners, endless product alternatives — optimisation is a rational strategy. The fear of settling is not irrational in an environment designed to surface the next option continuously.

**The Friction:** The infinite pool is not neutral — it is constructed. [[Recommender Systems]] — algorithms that determine what you see — are designed to surface more, not to help you commit to enough. [[Comparison Culture]] — the structural environment of continuous upward comparison — applies not just to appearance but to choices: is this the best restaurant, the best person, the best decision? [[Curated Life]] content amplifies this: seeing others' apparently optimal choices makes your own feel provisional. The paralysis is not a personality flaw. It is the logical outcome of an environment engineered to keep you searching.

**Why This Matters:** **FOBO** names the cost of infinite optionality. Once you see it as a structural feature rather than a personal weakness, the inability to decide becomes a different kind of problem — one that can be addressed structurally, not just individually.
