# Digital Coach


***She helps people navigate systems that were not designed to be navigated.***

**Literal meaning:** A **digital coach** is a person who helps others navigate digital systems — devices, apps, online services, security settings — through patient, personalised guidance rather than technical instruction. The role bridges the gap between how digital systems are designed and how non-expert users actually experience them.

**Origin:** The profession formalised in the 2010s as digital service delivery replaced physical service points at scale and as older adults, low-income users, and people with low digital literacy found themselves excluded from essential services. Libraries, community centres, and local government began employing digital coaches. In the Netherlands, the national Digisterker programme and various local digital inclusion initiatives created formal **digital coach** roles. Informally, the role has always existed — in every family with a technically literate younger member explaining things to older relatives.

> A profession that bridges the gap between how digital systems are designed and how people without technical expertise actually experience them.

**The Appeal:** Digital coaching works. Research on digital inclusion programmes consistently finds that one-to-one, patient, non-judgmental support produces better outcomes than training courses, written guides, or telephone helplines. The relationship between coach and learner allows for the kind of adaptive, personalised help that digital systems themselves rarely provide.

**The Friction:** The profession's existence is evidence of design failure. Every person who needs a **digital coach** is a person for whom the system was not designed. [[Digital Divide]] — structural inequality in digital access and skills — is what the **digital coach** addresses at the individual level while leaving the structural cause untouched. [[Ageism (Digital)]] — age discrimination in interface design — is one upstream cause: systems designed without older users produce the need for coaches to bridge the gap. The informal version of the role — the family member who helps — is largely performed by women and is unrecognised as labour. The formal version is paid but often precarious.

**Why This Matters:** **Digital coach** names the human response to systemic design failure. Once you see the profession as an indicator — more coaches needed means more people excluded — the question shifts from "how do we train more coaches?" to "why does the design keep producing the same exclusions?"
