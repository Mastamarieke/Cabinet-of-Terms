***The AI labelled the image. A human in the Philippines labelled the image. The AI got the credit.***

**Literal meaning**
*Mechanical Turk* — a famous 18th-century chess automaton that appeared to play autonomously but concealed a human chess master inside. Amazon's **Mechanical Turk** (MTurk) is a crowdsourcing platform that names itself after this deception.

**Origin**
Amazon launched MTurk in 2005, offering businesses access to a global on-demand workforce for "Human Intelligence Tasks" — microtasks that algorithms cannot yet perform: image labelling, content moderation, data verification. Hara et al. (2018) documented that median wages on the platform are well below minimum wage in any jurisdiction. The name — after a device designed to make human labour invisible — is analytically exact.

> Amazon's crowdsourcing platform for human microlabour — named after a historical deception it exactly reproduces: human intelligence presented as machine intelligence.

**The Appeal**
MTurk provides genuine income and flexible work for participants globally, and enables AI development that would otherwise be impossible or prohibitively expensive.

**The Friction**
Hara et al. (2018) document earnings averaging $2/hour with no labour protections, sick pay, or appeal mechanisms. Shapiro (2024) frames MTurk as a "digital sweatshop" that stripped workers of all protections except those they built themselves. [[Ghost Work]] is the broader category: invisible human labour that keeps AI systems functional while being structurally erased from the product narrative.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see **Mechanical Turk** as a labour infrastructure rather than a technology platform, you see that the AI systems presented as autonomous are dependent on an invisible, poorly paid, unprotected global workforce.
