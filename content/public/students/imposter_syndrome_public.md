# Imposter Syndrome

Everyone else seems to know what they are doing. You feel like you are one question away from being found out.

**Literal meaning**
*Imposter* — someone pretending to be something they are not. *Syndrome* — a recognised pattern of experience. Imposter Syndrome is the persistent feeling of being a fraud despite evidence of competence.

**Origin**
Psychologists Pauline Clance and Suzanne Imes first described the phenomenon in 1978, studying high-achieving women who felt their success was undeserved. The concept expanded to describe a widely shared experience across genders and disciplines. It gained new urgency as social media created permanent visibility of others' curated achievements.

> Imposter Syndrome is the persistent experience of feeling fraudulent despite competence — amplified by platform environments that display only the highlight reels of others' success.

**The Appeal**
Naming the experience is genuinely useful: recognising that the feeling is common and not evidence of actual incompetence reduces its power.

**The Friction**
[[public/students/personal_branding_public|Personal Branding]] — constructing a polished public identity — means everyone's visible self is curated. The comparison that produces imposter syndrome is between your private experience and others' public performance. [[public/students/hustle_culture_public|Hustle Culture]] amplifies it: if constant productivity is the norm, any moment of uncertainty feels like failure.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see imposter syndrome as partly a structural response to platform environments rather than purely individual psychology, you see that the solution is not only individual confidence-building — it is changing what gets made visible.
