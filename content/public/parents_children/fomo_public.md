# FOMO

The phone lights up. You do not check it immediately. But you wonder what you are missing. That wondering is the product.

**Literal meaning:** FOMO — Fear of Missing Out — is the anxiety or unease produced by the perception that others are having experiences, accessing opportunities, or participating in social events from which one is absent. As a digital phenomenon, it describes the state produced by continuous awareness of others' activities through social media: the sense that life is happening elsewhere, that the feed contains things you will have missed, that your absence from a platform or group has a social cost.

**Origin:** The term was coined by marketing strategist Dan Herman in 1996 and popularised by Patrick McGinnis in a 2004 Harvard Business School publication. It entered mainstream vocabulary in the early 2010s as smartphones and social media created the first environments in which real-time awareness of others' activities was continuous and ambient. JOMO — Joy of Missing Out — emerged as a deliberate counter-framing from around 2012.

> The anxiety of perceived social absence — a psychological state that platform architectures are specifically designed to produce and maintain, because users who fear missing out do not log off.

**The Appeal:** FOMO names something real. Social belonging is a fundamental human need, and the perception of exclusion produces genuine distress. In adolescent development — when peer belonging is a primary concern — this is intensified. The term gives a name to an experience that many people recognise immediately, and naming it can be the first step toward managing it.

**The Friction:** The platform's interest in FOMO is structural. [[Predatory Design]] — design maximising engagement — relies on users believing that absence has a cost. Notification systems, read receipts, activity indicators, and real-time updates are all design choices that make presence feel necessary and absence feel costly. [[Highlight Reel]] — showing only the best of others' lives — intensifies the perceived gap between one's own experience and what is being missed. [[Comparison Culture]] — permanent comparison as a platform effect — is FOMO's social mechanism. [[JOMO]] — Joy of Missing Out — is the appropriated counter-response: framed as resistance, it circulates on the same platforms that produce the anxiety it addresses. For children and adolescents, whose developmental stage makes them particularly sensitive to social exclusion, FOMO is not a personality trait — it is a designed-in response to an environment engineered to produce it.

**Why This Matters:** FOMO names an anxiety and obscures its manufacture. Once you know the design choices that produce it, the fear of missing out becomes legible as an intended outcome rather than an incidental one.
