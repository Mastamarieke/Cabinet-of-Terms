# Gaming Disorder

The WHO gave it a name. The gaming industry said it did not exist. The child could not stop playing.

**Literal meaning:** Gaming disorder is a clinical classification introduced by the World Health Organisation in ICD-11 (2018), describing a pattern of gaming behaviour characterised by impaired control over gaming, increasing priority given to gaming over other activities, and continuation of gaming despite negative consequences — persisting for at least twelve months and causing significant impairment in personal, family, social, educational, or occupational functioning.

**Origin:** The classification was contested from the moment it was proposed. The gaming industry lobbied against its inclusion in ICD-11, arguing that evidence was insufficient and that pathologising gaming would stigmatise normal behaviour. Many researchers agreed that the evidence base was weak and that the category risked medicalising the consequences of poor game design rather than individual pathology. The WHO proceeded, citing clinical utility and the need for a framework that health professionals could use.

> A WHO clinical classification for pathological gaming behaviour — which names a real pattern of harm while leaving open the question of whether the disorder is in the person or in the product.

**The Appeal:** Gaming disorder gives clinicians, parents, and individuals a framework for recognising and treating a genuine pattern of distress. Children and families seeking help with gaming-related harm need clinical language to access services, justify concern, and frame treatment. The classification creates that access. It also generates research funding and public health attention.

**The Friction:** The classification frames the problem as residing in the individual — their behaviour, their impaired control, their disorder. [[Predatory Design]] — design that deliberately exploits developmental vulnerabilities — is the upstream cause that the diagnosis does not name. A child whose gaming behaviour is pathological in the clinical sense may be responding to products that were engineered to produce exactly the compulsive engagement they are experiencing. [[Dopamine Feedback Loops]] — variable reward systems — are the designed mechanism. [[Loot Boxes]] add financial compulsion. The disorder is real; the question is whether it is primarily a feature of the person or of the product. The classification, by locating it in the person, leaves the product out of the treatment plan.

**Why This Matters:** Gaming disorder teaches a diagnostic reading practice: ask not only what is wrong with this person, but what is this person responding to. When a designed product produces a clinically recognised disorder in a significant number of its users, that is also information about the product.
