# Sharenting

Before the child can speak, they have a digital footprint. Someone else built it.

**Literal meaning:** Sharenting — a portmanteau of *sharing* and *parenting* — describes the practice of parents sharing photographs, videos, and personal information about their children on social media platforms. The term covers everything from occasional holiday photos to systematic, daily documentation of a child's life as content.

**Origin:** The term was coined around 2012 and gained broad currency through journalism and parenting discourse in the mid-2010s. It emerged as social media platforms became the primary infrastructure for sharing family life, and as the volume and intimacy of child-related content shared online grew beyond anything previous generations of parents had encountered as a cultural norm.

> The practice of parents sharing images and information about their children online — creating a digital identity for a person who cannot consent to it.

**The Appeal:** Sharenting meets real needs. Grandparents see grandchildren grow up across distances. Parents find community, recognition, and support in sharing the realities of raising children. Documentation feels like care — preserving moments that would otherwise be lost. For many parents, there is nothing unusual about it: this is simply how contemporary family life is shared.

**The Friction:** The child cannot consent. By the time they are old enough to have a view on their digital presence, it is already extensive, indexed, and in many cases permanent. [[Predatory Design]] — design choices aimed at maximising engagement — rewards emotional content about children: vulnerability, cuteness, and milestones generate high engagement. The platform's interest and the parent's impulse to share align — but the third party, the child, is not in the negotiation. [[Momfluencer]] — mother as content producer with child as protagonist — is the professional form of the same logic; sharenting is its everyday version. [[Online Grooming]] — the use of platforms to establish inappropriate relationships with minors — is one documented risk of high-visibility child content: detailed, geotagged, routine documentation of a child's life can be used by people with harmful intent. The digital footprint created belongs to the child but was built by the parent.

**Why This Matters:** Sharenting makes visible a consent gap that did not exist before social media: a child's digital identity is constructed years before they can participate in, contest, or delete it. Once you know the term, a parent's Instagram post of their child is also a data point, a footprint, and a decision made on someone else's behalf.
