# Sexting

The legal framework was written for adults. The behaviour it criminalises is common among teenagers. The law does not always distinguish between the two.

**Literal meaning:** Sexting — a portmanteau of *sex* and *texting* — refers to the sending, receiving, or forwarding of sexually explicit or intimate images, video, or text via digital communication tools. As a phenomenon involving minors, it occupies contested territory between adolescent developmental behaviour, child protection law, and platform responsibility.

**Origin:** The term emerged in the mid-2000s with the proliferation of camera phones and messaging applications, and entered public and legal discourse widely from around 2009. Its legal complexity became apparent early: in many jurisdictions, a teenager sending a self-image to a peer could technically be prosecuted under child pornography statutes designed for adult abusers — a mismatch that led to legislative reform in some jurisdictions and prosecutorial discretion in others. Research from the 2010s onward documented that sexting between peers of similar age was common in adolescent populations across many countries.

> The digital transmission of intimate content involving minors — a behaviour at the intersection of adolescent development, peer pressure, platform access, and legal frameworks designed for a different context.

**The Appeal:** Adolescent sexual development involves curiosity, experimentation, and peer communication about intimacy. In previous generations, some of this happened through notes, photographs, or conversation; digital communication is the current medium. Between consenting peers of similar age, some researchers argue that sexting is a contemporary extension of developmental behaviour that criminalising unduly pathologises adolescence.

**The Friction:** The power dynamics are the critical variable. Between peers, the risk profile differs from sexting under coercion — whether from a peer, an adult, or the designed social pressures of a platform. [[Online Grooming]] uses sexting as both a stage in and an instrument of abuse: groomers normalise intimate image exchange and use received images as leverage. [[Predatory Design]] creates the conditions: platforms that allow private messaging between minors and adults, without adequate age verification or monitoring, are infrastructure for coercion. [[Deepfake]] technology extends the harm: images shared consensually can be used to generate non-consensual synthetic content. The legal framework's bluntness — treating all intimate imagery involving minors as equivalent regardless of context — produces injustice at both ends: underprosecuting adult abusers in some cases, overprosecuting adolescent peers in others.

**Why This Matters:** Sexting makes visible the gap between adolescent developmental reality, legal frameworks, and platform design responsibility. All three are operating in the same space with different logics — and children are in the middle of the mismatch.
