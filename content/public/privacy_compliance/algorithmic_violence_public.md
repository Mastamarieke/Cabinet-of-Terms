# Algorithmic Violence

The algorithm denied the benefit. No one decided. No one is responsible. The harm is real.

**Literal meaning**
*Algorithmic* — produced by automated computational processes. *Violence* — the exercise of force or power that causes harm. Algorithmic Violence describes harm produced by automated systems — not as an accident but as a structural outcome.

**Origin**
The term emerged from critical algorithm studies and digital rights scholarship, associated with researchers including Safiya Umoja Noble and Virginia Eubanks. It describes documented harms: welfare algorithms that wrongly deny benefits, predictive policing systems that target communities of colour, content moderation systems that remove marginalised voices.

> Algorithmic Violence is harm produced by automated systems — real damage without individual decision-making, distributed across populations without accountability.

**The Appeal**
Automated systems promise consistency, efficiency, and freedom from human bias. The appeal is genuine: human decision-makers are also biased, often more so.

**The Friction**
[[public/platform_mechanisms/recommender_systems_public|Recommender Systems]] — optimising for engagement — can constitute algorithmic violence when they amplify content that targets, harasses, or endangers specific groups. The harm is structural: no individual chose to harm; the system produces harm as an output. Accountability is diffuse — engineers, managers, and platform owners all contributed, but no one decided.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see algorithmic violence as a structural category rather than a system error, you see that the question of accountability cannot be resolved by fixing bugs — it requires asking who benefits from the system's outputs and who bears the costs.
