# Greenwashing

A tech company announces it will be carbon neutral by 2030. Its data centre construction programme doubles the same week.

**Literal meaning:** Greenwashing is the practice of presenting a product, company, or policy as environmentally responsible through communication — without making substantive changes to underlying practices. The "wash" in the term alludes to whitewashing: covering over rather than cleaning.

**Origin:** The term was coined by environmentalist Jay Westerveld in 1986 in response to hotel towel-reuse campaigns — small gestures of sustainability deployed to distract from far larger environmental impacts. It has since become a standard term in corporate accountability discourse and, from the early 2020s, has become central to AI energy debates as major tech companies began publishing sustainability pledges alongside rapidly expanding infrastructure.

> Deploying environmental language and commitments as reputation management, without proportional change to the practices causing environmental harm.

**The Appeal:** Sustainability pledges serve a real function: they create public accountability, attract talent who care about the environment, and sometimes produce genuine efficiency gains. Carbon offset markets, renewable energy certificates, and efficiency improvements are not always empty. Inside organisations, the sustainability team may be entirely sincere.

**The Friction:** The problem is structural. [[AI Energy Consumption]] is growing faster than renewable energy capacity can absorb it. A company can be simultaneously carbon neutral on paper — through offsets and certificates — and responsible for rapidly increasing absolute emissions. [[Externalized Costs]] — environmental burdens shifted onto communities rather than absorbed by the company — are not captured by carbon accounting. What is measured is chosen; what is not measured does not appear in the sustainability report. The language of responsibility becomes the instrument of its own evasion.

**Why This Matters:** Once you know greenwashing, a press release is never only a press release. The question to ask is not *what does the company claim* but *what does the claim not include*. Pledges set in the future are not actions in the present. The gap between those two sentences is where scrutiny lives.
