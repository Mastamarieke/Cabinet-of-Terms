# AI Energy Consumption

A data centre can consume as much electricity as a mid-sized city — and the demand is accelerating.

**Literal meaning:** The total electrical energy required to build, run, and query AI systems — from training large models to answering individual user requests.

**Origin:** The term gained analytical traction after a 2019 study by Emma Strubell and colleagues at the University of Massachusetts quantified the carbon cost of training a single large language model, comparing it to the lifetime emissions of five cars. Since then, as models have grown dramatically larger, energy has moved from a footnote to a central controversy in AI development.

> AI systems require continuous, large-scale electricity consumption at every stage — from initial training to each individual query — with costs that are rarely visible to the user.

**The Appeal:** For researchers, developers, and companies, energy consumption is simply the price of capability. More compute means better models, and better models mean more accurate outputs, medical breakthroughs, scientific progress. The electricity bill feels like an engineering problem with an engineering solution.

**The Friction:** The costs do not disappear — they are transferred. [[Externalized Costs]] — environmental burdens borne by communities rather than reflected in the price of AI services — mean that the person asking a chatbot a question does not pay the energy bill. A community near a data centre does. [[Sacrifice Zones]] — areas bearing disproportionate environmental burdens of digital infrastructure — are often low-income or already environmentally stressed. The abstraction of the cloud makes this transfer invisible by design.

**Why This Matters:** Once you know this term, a simple question changes: *whose electricity is this?* The fluency and speed of an AI response starts to carry weight. Not moral paralysis — but a new kind of legibility. The invisible is made locatable.
