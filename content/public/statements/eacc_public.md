# e/acc (Effective Accelerationism)

Some people believe the solution to the risks of powerful technology is to build it faster. Not slower. Faster.

**Literal meaning:** Effective accelerationism — abbreviated e/acc — is an ideological movement that holds that technological and economic acceleration is not only inevitable but morally desirable. It is explicitly opposed to AI safety and slowdown movements, arguing that attempts to regulate or slow technological development are both futile and harmful. The "effective" prefix borrows the branding of effective altruism while inverting its caution about existential risk.

**Origin:** The movement crystallised on social media around 2022–2023, associated with figures including Beff Jezos (pseudonym of Guillaume Verdon) and gaining visibility when prominent Silicon Valley investors and founders publicly identified with it. It draws on Nick Land's accelerationist philosophy from the 1990s — the idea that capitalism and technology have their own momentum that should be embraced rather than resisted — stripped of Land's more dystopian framing and repackaged as optimism.

> The belief that technological acceleration is inherently good and should be maximised — that safety concerns, regulation, and slowdown are moral failures rather than prudent responses.

**The Appeal:** e/acc offers a coherent response to paralysis. If powerful technology is coming regardless, the argument goes, it is better to accelerate it under current, relatively open conditions than to slow it until it arrives under more authoritarian ones. The movement also captures something genuine: excessive caution in technology development has real costs, and some AI safety discourse does shade into catastrophism that forecloses legitimate uses.

**The Friction:** The ideology functions as a permission structure. If acceleration is inherently moral, then scrutiny of specific harms — labour displacement, environmental cost, concentration of power — becomes a form of moral failure. [[Armageddon Complex]] — the use of apocalyptic thinking to justify power accumulation — describes the rhetorical move: the stakes are so high and the trajectory so inevitable that ordinary accountability does not apply. [[Doomerism]] — the fatalistic belief that technological disaster is inevitable — is e/acc's mirror image: both positions remove the possibility of deliberate, collective choice about technological direction. [[Millenarianism]] — end-times thinking as political-religious structure — is the deeper cultural substrate both share.

**Why This Matters:** e/acc makes visible a specific move in technology discourse: dressing ideological preference as historical necessity. "It's inevitable" is never a neutral observation — it is always an argument for who should be in charge of the inevitable.
