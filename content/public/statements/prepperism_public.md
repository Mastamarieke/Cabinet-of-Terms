# Prepperism

Somewhere between sensible emergency planning and full civilisational exit, prepperism has become a culture, a market, and an identity.

**Literal meaning:** Prepperism — from *prepper*, a person who prepares for disasters — is the practice and identity of systematically preparing for civilisational disruption: stockpiling food, water, medicine, and supplies; learning survival skills; acquiring weapons and communication tools; and planning for scenarios ranging from natural disaster to societal collapse. It exists on a broad spectrum from pragmatic emergency readiness to elaborate bunker communities organised around specific collapse scenarios.

**Origin:** Prepper culture has roots in Cold War civil defence, American frontier mythology, and religious survivalism. It gained mainstream visibility in the United States in the 2000s and 2010s, amplified by reality television, online communities, and supply chains selling survival products. The 2020 pandemic brought elements of preparedness thinking into mainstream discourse. Across this period, prepperism developed as a distinct identity — not just a set of practices but a worldview and community membership.

> The practice and identity of individual or small-group preparation for civilisational disruption — where survival readiness becomes a culture organised around the expectation of collective failure.

**The Appeal:** At its most basic, prepperism addresses a genuine gap: most people are genuinely underprepared for even minor disruptions — a week without power, a supply chain interruption. Emergency preparedness is prudent, governments recommend it, and the skills involved — first aid, food preservation, communication without infrastructure — are valuable. The community dimension provides belonging and practical knowledge exchange.

**The Friction:** The identity dimension is where prepperism diverges from emergency planning. Survival as identity project organises life around the expectation of collapse — which shapes perception, politics, and social relationships in ways that preparedness alone does not. [[Millenarianism]] — the deep structure of end-times thinking — is the cultural substrate: the world is failing, those who are prepared will survive, and community is constituted by shared knowledge of what others deny. [[Doomsday Prep for the Super-Rich]] is the same logic at a different economic scale — and the comparison reveals something: the billionaire bunker and the backyard stockpile share a structural logic of individual exit that forecloses collective response. [[Doomerism]] — the fatalistic belief that disaster is inevitable — is prepperism's emotional core, even when the prepper insists they are being practical.

**Why This Matters:** Prepperism makes visible how civilisational anxiety gets channelled into consumer identity rather than political action. Buying more supplies is easier than changing the systems that make supply chains fragile. The market for prepper gear depends on the anxiety it addresses remaining unresolved.
