# Digital Coach

In most families, there is someone who explains the new phone, resets the router, and sits patiently while a parent tries to log in for the fourth time. That person has a profession. It just is not paid.

**Literal meaning:** A digital coach is a person — formal or informal — who helps others navigate digital systems: setting up devices, understanding platforms, managing privacy settings, troubleshooting problems, and building confidence with technology. Formal digital coaching exists in public libraries, community centres, and some commercial contexts. The much larger informal version is performed, usually without payment, by digitally literate individuals for family members, neighbours, and community members.

**Origin:** The role emerged as a recognisable pattern in the 2000s and 2010s, as digital systems became mandatory infrastructure for healthcare, banking, government services, and social connection — while remaining inaccessible to significant portions of the population. Formal digital coaching programmes developed in public institutions; the informal role simply expanded wherever the gap between system complexity and user capacity was not bridged by design.

> A person — paid or unpaid — who helps others navigate digital systems that were not designed with those users in mind.

**The Appeal:** Digital coaching is genuinely helpful. The knowledge transfer is real, the confidence gained is lasting, and the social connection involved — patient, one-to-one assistance — is valued by both parties. Formal digital coaching programmes have documented outcomes: older adults who complete them report reduced isolation, increased confidence, and improved access to services. The role works.

**The Friction:** The digital coach is a compensatory role — it exists because systems were not designed accessibly. [[Digital Exclusion]] — active exclusion of people by design or policy — is what the digital coach partially mitigates. [[Digital Ageing]] — growing older in a digital society as a condition rather than a choice — is the structural context: people are not failing to keep up with technology; technology is being designed without them. [[Technostress]] — stress caused by digital systems — is what the digital coach reduces at the individual level, without addressing the design choices that produce it. The informal version of the role is also a quietly significant transfer of unpaid labour — predominantly from younger to older family members, predominantly performed by women — that is invisible in any accounting of digital infrastructure's social costs.

**Why This Matters:** The digital coach makes visible a design debt: every person who needs coaching is evidence of a system that failed to include them. The profession should not need to exist at this scale. That it does is a measurement.
