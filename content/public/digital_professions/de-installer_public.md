# De-installer

Someone has to know which apps should not be on your phone, how to get them off, and why they came back after you thought you removed them.

**Literal meaning:** A de-installer is a person — formal or informal — who helps others remove harmful, addictive, or predatory applications from their devices. The role is not primarily technical: it requires knowledge of which apps are harmful, why they resist removal, what replaces them, and how to support the person through the social consequences of disconnecting from platforms their community uses.

**Origin:** The role has no single origin point — it emerged organically as a protective practice within families, schools, and communities dealing with problem technology use. It appears in digital literacy education, in youth work, in family therapy contexts, and as an informal role taken on by digitally literate individuals in their social networks. It is almost never paid.

> A person who helps others remove harmful or addictive apps from their devices — a role that only exists because those apps were designed to be difficult to leave.

**The Appeal:** De-installation is concrete. Unlike digital literacy education, which operates in the abstract, de-installation addresses a specific, immediate problem: this app is harming this person, and it needs to go. The role is practical, effective in the short term, and genuinely helpful to people who lack the knowledge or confidence to act alone.

**The Friction:** The de-installer exists because [[Predatory Design]] — design choices aimed at maximising engagement and spending — makes removal difficult by design. Unsubscribe flows are hidden. Notifications resume after deletion. Social pressure to remain on a platform is built into its architecture. The de-installer is a workaround for a design failure that platforms have no incentive to fix. [[Dark Patterns]] — interface design that misleads users — are the specific mechanisms the de-installer learns to navigate. The role is unpaid, informal, and structurally reactive: it cannot keep pace with the platforms it addresses. [[Cognitive Surrender]] — the gradual transfer of agency to digital systems — is what the de-installer tries to partially reverse, one device at a time.

**Why This Matters:** The de-installer makes visible the gap between how platforms present themselves — free services, optional participation — and how they function: systems designed to resist exit. A profession that exists to undo what another profession built is a signal worth reading.
