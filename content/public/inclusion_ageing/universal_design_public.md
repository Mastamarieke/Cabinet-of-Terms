# Universal Design

The curb cut was designed for wheelchair users. Everyone uses it — cyclists, parents with prams, delivery workers. Designing for the margin improved for the centre.

**Literal meaning**
*Universal* — applicable to all. *Design* — the intentional structuring of systems and artefacts. Universal Design is the principle that products and environments should be usable by all people, to the greatest extent possible, without adaptation.

**Origin**
Ron Mace coined Universal Design in 1988 at North Carolina State University. The concept emerged from disability rights and architecture, proposing that accessible design should not be a retrofit but a starting condition. The "curb cut effect" became its paradigm case: design for disability that improves usability for everyone.

> Universal Design is the design principle that systems should be usable by all people from the outset — not adapted for specific groups after the fact.

**The Appeal**
Universal Design reduces cost: building in accessibility from the start is cheaper than retrofitting. It also improves products for all users — the curb cut effect is documented across domains.

**The Friction**
Universal Design is a principle, not a guarantee. Applied without genuine user research involving diverse users, it can produce designs that claim universality while still excluding. [[public/design_philosophy/vsd_value_sensitive_design_public|VSD]] — systematically integrating human values into design — is the methodological complement: Universal Design states the goal; VSD provides tools for achieving it.

**Why This Matters**
Once you see Universal Design as a design philosophy rather than a technical standard, you see that the question is not whether a design is compliant — it is whether the people who need it most were involved in making it.
